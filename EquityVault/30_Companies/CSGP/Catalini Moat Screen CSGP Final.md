**Section 3 — Business Overview**

**What CoStar Sells**

CoStar Group is a real estate information, analytics, marketplace, and
3D digital twin technology company organized into **two reporting
segments** since the Q4 2025 reorganization: **Commercial** ($1.79B
FY25 revenue, +18% YoY) and **Residential** ($1.46B, +20% reported /
+12% organic).

The Commercial segment comprises CoStar Suite (the flagship CRE database
with ~300,000 subscribers and 94% quarterly renewal), LoopNet (CRE
listings marketplace), Real Estate Manager / Visual Lease (lease
administration SaaS, acquired late 2024), STR (hotel performance
benchmarking with customer-contributed data), Matterport (3D capture),
and CoStar Debt Solutions (CRE lending workflow). Per the FY2025 10-K,
this is a 35+ year accumulation of property data, transaction
comparables, and market analytics, supported by *"the largest commercial
real estate research department in the industry"* — approximately 1,600
human researchers per expert practitioner sources.

The Residential segment comprises Apartments.com (US apartment
marketplace; $1.25B FY25 revenue with 99% monthly property manager
renewal and 89,275 paying properties), Homes.com (residential home
search; ~$100M ARR with 31,000 agent subscribers), Domain (Australia,
acquired Aug 2025), OnTheMarket (UK), and Land.com. Apartments.com is
the dominant US apartment marketplace with 67% brand awareness vs. 36%
for the #2 platform. Homes.com is the contested challenger to Zillow
and Realtor.com in residential home search.

**Competitive Landscape**

CSGP's reorganization into two segments (Commercial / Residential)

reference-standard information product with switching costs and data
gravity; Residential is a two-sided marketplace with network effects.
The framework is applied separately to each segment because the
mechanics differ, then synthesized in the composite section.

**Section 4 — Moat-by-Moat Analysis**

**4.1 — System of Record / Switching Costs (Commercial) — PRIMARY**

**Classification:** CoStar Suite is best characterized as a
**reference-standard analytical SoR with embedded coordination
equilibrium** — not a deeply-configured operational SoR in the
SAP/Oracle Financials sense. The platform is the *"training curriculum
for the CRE industry"* (per the 10-K Item 1 framing): junior analysts
are taught to use CoStar as part of onboarding at brokerages, REITs, and
lenders. The retention is driven by two load-bearing dimensions —
counterparty coordination and learning/retraining cost — both of which
are non-measurable in the Catalini sense and therefore agent-resistant.
The other switching-cost dimensions (data migration, workflow
customization, integration ecosystem) are real but weaker, and most are
at least partially automatable by AI agents on a 3-7 year horizon.

**Classical Strength**

|  |  |  |
|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **Dimension** | **Score** | **Evidence** |
| Contract stickiness | 3.0 | Annual contracts the norm; 94% quarterly renewal (Q4 2025) but seat counts and module mix not stable. Pressure test reveals customers renewing at lower price points (see bifurcation discussion). |
| Data migration friction | 3.0 | Real friction today (saved searches, custom reports, citations) but agent-automatable. "They are canceling their six-figure CoStar contract to shift their workflows to Claude Cowork" (Founder & Partner, Triad Real Estate Partners, Apr 2026). |
| Learning / retraining cost | 5.0 | Load-bearing. CoStar IS the training curriculum. Industry-wide retraining is enormous and not agent-automatable — the apprenticeship is human-to-human. |
| Workflow customization | 3.0 | Custom reports, integrations, and saved analyses exist but are moderately portable. Not a deeply-configured product like Salesforce. |
| Integration ecosystem | 3.0 | MRI/Yardi/Argus integrations and CRE CRM connectors. Real but replicable; integrations are read-only data pull rather than two-way workflow. |
| Compliance lock-in | 1.0 | Near zero. No regulatory mandate; no auditor requirement. Stark contrast to genuine enterprise SoRs with SOX or HIPAA anchoring. |
| Counterparty coordination (Schelling point) | 5.0 | Load-bearing. "100% of commercial real estate brokers use CoStar... an absolute requirement for sharing contact info and transaction history" (Managing Director at Cushman & Wakefield, Henry.ai expert call, Dec 2025). Institutional clients in RFPs "specifically expect to see CoStar data" (VP at Asset Living, Jan 2026). |
| Customer-data integration | 3.0 | Larger enterprise customers have built analytical pipelines on CoStar feeds; smaller customers minimal. Selective lock-in. |

**Classical Score: 4.0 / 5.0** — weighted toward the two load-bearing
dimensions (learning cost and coordination equilibrium) which
empirically explain the observed retention. Arithmetic mean across the 8
dimensions is 3.3; the weighted view of 4.0 reflects the analytical
judgment that the retention is overwhelmingly explained by the two
highest-scoring dimensions.

**Bifurcation: Category A (Migration Labor) vs. Category B
(Coordination/Context)**

The Catalini framework decomposes switching costs into Category A —
measurable migration labor that AI agents can compress — and Category B
— coordination and context-based costs that agents cannot replicate
because they emerge from social and institutional consensus.

|  |  |  |  |
|:--:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **Component** | **Category** | **Auto-able %** | **Detail** |
| Saved searches, custom reports, workspace configs | Cat. A | 100% | Already compressing — Triad case shows boutique firms substituting Claude at $20/month for six-figure contracts |
| Data pipeline integrations (MRI/Yardi/Argus) | Cat. A | ~80% | Mostly automatable migration work; small Cat. B residue in custom-developed integrations |
| User retraining cost | Cat. B | 100% | Not automatable — human-to-human apprenticeship; learning is the binding constraint at the analyst level |
| Citation continuity in reports/memos | Cat. B | 100% | Coordination equilibrium — counterparties expect CoStar citations regardless of whether the underlying data could be replaced |
| Counterparty Schelling point | Cat. B | 100% | Institutional. "100% of commercial real estate brokers use CoStar." CMBS underwriters use CoStar "similar to how they use Moody's" (Former VP at CoStar Group, Nov 2025) |
| Workflow customization | Mixed | 60% A / 40% B | The technical customization is automatable; the workflow norms are coordination-based |

**Aggregate: ~55% Category A / ~45% Category B**. The Category B portion
(coordination, learning, citation continuity) is durable and is the
load-bearing portion of the moat. Category A (migration labor, data
pipeline rebuilding, workspace configuration) is already actively
compressing for boutique firms — "AI agents are beginning to replace
specific CoStar workflows" with measurable cost compression observed in
2025-2026 (Founder & Partner, Triad Real Estate Partners, Apr 2026). The
compression timeline on Category A is **3-7 years for the mid-market;
institutional-tier customers more durable on a 7-10 year horizon**
because the Category B coordination component holds even as Category A
erodes.

**4.2 — Ground Truth / Data (Commercial) — PRIMARY**

**Classification:** CoStar's data asset is **not a unitary moat** — it
bifurcates by product. The pressure test surfaced segment-level
granularity (April 21, 2026 transcript: Former Sr. Sales Executive at
CoStar Group) that fundamentally changes the durability picture.
Multifamily Commercial Data is highly vulnerable to AI replication; Core
Commercial Data (office, retail, industrial brokerage) is materially
more defensible than the original screen credited; STR (hotel
benchmarks) and Private Bank loan data are verification-grade
peer-contribution networks. The data IS the product, generated by a
1,600-researcher field operation using drones with military-grade aerial
photography — described by a former executive as *"like trying to
rebuild Disney World"* — and protected by copyright on 35 million
proprietary images. The sell-side framing of "continuously verified
dataset... largely resilient to AI disruption" (Citi, April 2026) is
partially right (Core Commercial holds) and partially wrong (Multifamily
Data is being replaced today).

give-to-get private bank data network. These are infrastructure moats,
not just data moats.

**Bifurcation: Execution-Grade vs. Verification-Grade —
Product-Weighted**

The most consequential analytical move in the Catalini framework is
distinguishing **execution-grade data** (finished records of what
happened — vulnerable as frontier models converge) from
**verification-grade data** (failure logs, dispute histories, edge-case
resolutions, audit trails — durable because idiosyncratic and invisible
in public data). The original screen scored CoStar at a unitary 70%
execution / 30% verification. The pressure test reveals this was
directionally too pessimistic for the core because the bifurcation is
product-specific:

|  |  |  |  |
|:--:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **Segment** | **Exec-Grade %** | **Verif-Grade %** | **Detail** |
| Multifamily Commercial Data | 85% | 15% | "Apartment data is widely available on public property websites and agency domains... HelloData are already successfully using AI to scrape property websites and duplicate this data" (Former Sr. Sales Executive, Apr 2026). Highly vulnerable; AI replacement active TODAY. |
| Core Commercial Data (office/retail/industrial) | 50% | 50% | Researchers manually call brokers; physical research with drones; aerial photography; legal protection on imagery. "Very big stretch for AI to replicate." "Like trying to rebuild Disney World" (Former Director at CoStar Group, Jun 2025). Verification-grade infrastructure. |
| LoopNet (advertising) | n/a | n/a | Marketplace, not data — separate moat scoring under Network Effects. Brokers actively choose to pay; AI cannot "take over" a workflow that requires direct broker action. |
| STR (hotel benchmarks) | 30% | 70% | Customer-contributed peer benchmark — "incredibly difficult to compile and research hotel metrics to STR's level of depth simply by attempting to scrape consumer-facing sites like Booking.com" (Former Sr. Sales Executive, Apr 2026). Network effect. |
| Debt Solutions — CMBS Data | 85% | 15% | Replicable — CoStar "simply purchases commercial mortgage-backed securities (CMBS) data from third-party vendors like Intex." Trepp, CRED iQ, Bloomberg can buy the same data. |
| Debt Solutions — Private Bank Data | 30% | 70% | Highly defensible give-to-get network. "A new entrant would need to build a massive, trusted platform to convince individual banks to share their data directly, which is exceedingly difficult" (Former Sr. Sales Executive, Apr 2026). |

Verification-Grade**. This is materially more verification-grade than
the original 70/30 unitary scoring because the dominant CoStar Suite
product (Core Commercial) is more defensible than initially credited,
and the verification-grade products (STR, Private Bank) are larger
contributors to the moat than to revenue. The convergence timeline on
the execution-grade portion is 3-7 years for Multifamily and CMBS; the
verification-grade portion is 7-10+ years and may extend further if
CoStar moves up the verification stack (data warranty, indemnification —
not yet in management's roadmap).

**Appendix B — Key Definitions & Sources**

**B.1 Key Definitions**

**Execution-Grade vs. Verification-Grade Data**

The most important bifurcation in the Catalini framework is between two
kinds of proprietary data, and it is also the hardest to intuit from the
terms alone. Two analogies may help.

***Michael Jordan.** When asked how he hit a baseline jumper under
pressure, Jordan answered: "I pivoted off my foot, jumped, and shot."
Pressed again — **how** did he do it — same answer. He isn't being
evasive. He's giving the most accurate description he can access. The
gap between his answer and what actually happened is the point: the
knowledge that made Jordan **Jordan** is structurally inarticulable. A
decade of watching him shoot wouldn't transfer it; that's why his
teammates never became him.*

***Todd Combs and Warren Buffett.** Combs, Berkshire's apprentice stock
picker hired in 2010, has described spending Saturdays at Buffett's
house working through valuation questions together. Buffett asks, for
instance, how many S&P 500 names will earn more in five years at a 90%
confidence interval — a rubric he and Combs have used to find several of
their most successful investments. You can read every 13F Berkshire has
ever filed. You cannot read the Saturday conversation. The 13Fs are the
**output**. The Saturday conversations are the **process** that produced
them — the rejections, the priors, the "that name keeps coming up," the
rubrics that have never been published.*

**Execution-grade data** is the 13F. It is the artifact of decisions
getting made — the finished code, the completed trade, the approved
loan, the shipped product. It teaches a system *what to do*. Valuable —
but as frontier models ingest more of this kind of data across all
industries, the marginal edge from owning any one firm's execution
artifacts compresses. This is the vulnerable kind of "data moat."

**Verification-grade data** is the Saturday conversation. The redlines,
the rejections, the reasons a senior practitioner killed a deal, the
near-miss that never became an incident, the "that number feels wrong"
instinct. It teaches a system *what to reject*. It exists as negative
space around the finished artifacts, is rarely captured as a reusable
asset, and when it *is* captured, it compounds — because each edge case
resolved becomes precedent for the next one.

**The concrete test:** *If your competitor had frontier AI plus all the
public data in your industry, what would they still not have? If the
answer is "our customers' workflows," you have execution-grade data —
vulnerable. If the answer is "a decade of audit trails, dispute logs,
and reasoned rejections," you have verification-grade data — durable.*

**CSGP application:** CoStar's data is product-weighted. Multifamily
Commercial Data is execution-grade (publicly available, AI-replicable).
Core Commercial brokerage data is mixed — the property records are
execution-grade, but the proprietary lease terms, transaction-level
participants, and CoStar's research-QC dispute history are
verification-grade ("like trying to rebuild Disney World"). STR hotel
benchmarks and Private Bank loan data are verification-grade
peer-contribution networks. The unitary 70/30 split was directionally
too pessimistic for the core; the product-weighted view is closer to
~60/40 execution/verification on a revenue basis.

**Coordination Equilibrium / Schelling Point**

A coordination equilibrium emerges when both sides of a market — or all
participants in an industry — coordinate on a shared standard not
because that standard is technically superior, but because each
participant expects the others to be there. The equilibrium is
self-reinforcing and path-dependent: once it's established, switching
costs include not just the technical migration but the
social-coordination cost of moving without your counterparties.
Schelling points are agent-resistant because agents cannot manufacture
mutual expectations. CSGP examples: CoStar Suite as the CRE reference
standard for institutional underwriting ("100% of CRE brokers use
CoStar"); Apartments.com as the US apartment-listing default (67% brand
awareness vs. 36% #2).

**Category A vs. Category B Switching Cost**

**Category A switching costs** are measurable execution work — saved
searches, custom report templates, data pipeline rebuilds, workspace
configurations, integration mappings. These are agent-automatable: the
work has observable inputs, observable outputs, and verifiable success
criteria. As AI agents become more capable, Category A switching costs
compress toward zero.

**Category B switching costs** are coordination and context-based —
counterparty Schelling points, learning/retraining cost, citation
continuity in professional workflows, institutional norms about what
data sources are accepted. These are agent-resistant because they emerge
from social and institutional consensus that no individual agent can
manufacture. The framework treats Category B as the durable portion of
any switching-cost moat.

**CSGP application:** CoStar Suite's switching costs are roughly 55%
Category A / 45% Category B. The Category B coordination component
("100% of CRE brokers use CoStar"; CMBS underwriters using it "similar
to how they use Moody's") is what carries the durable moat over a 7-10+
year horizon; the Category A portion is already actively compressing for
boutique firms (Triad, Avenue5).

**AI Sandwich**

A pattern in talent-moat erosion where AI tooling collapses traditional
career hierarchies — junior roles get automated away (the bottom of the
sandwich), senior roles operate at higher leverage with smaller teams,
and the apprenticeship pipeline that produced the next generation of
senior practitioners is broken. The risk is not that AI replaces senior
practitioners directly, but that the firm-scale efficient operating size
shrinks, eroding scale-based moats. Not directly relevant to CSGP —
talent is not the primary moat — but referenced for completeness.

**Codifier's Curse**

The phenomenon where AI tools that capture and codify expert judgment
progressively reduce the expert's leverage as the codified knowledge
becomes generally available. The expert effectively trains away their
own scarcity premium. Not directly applied to CSGP because the core moat
is data and coordination, not expert judgment, but referenced for
completeness.

**Competitive Advantage Period (CAP)**

The number of years of above-average competitive economics implied by a
stock's current price and consensus growth assumptions. Calculated by
working out how many years of high-multiple growth are needed before the
implied terminal multiple compresses to the equity-market average.
CSGP's current ~3-year CAP-to-Equal-Weight (calculated on Citi's FY26E
EPS of $1.32 at $38.91 stock price) is at the low end of the
framework's expected 3-7 year range for a Contested-with-Fortress-sleeve
stack.
