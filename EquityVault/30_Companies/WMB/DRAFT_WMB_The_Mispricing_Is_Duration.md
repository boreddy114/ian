## Summary

WMB has rerated from roughly $52 to $72 over the last twelve months on the Power Innovation behind-the-meter narrative. Sell-side price targets now sit in the $81–$91 range — RBC at $82, Jefferies at $81, UBS at $91 — implying 13% to 26% upside from spot. The question that brings us here: is the re-rate finished?

My answer is no, but the remaining alpha is structural rather than narrative. The market is pricing Williams as a midstream company with a fortunate data-center call option. The evidence points to something different — a Tier 6 coordination equilibrium with a 20–25+ year competitive advantage period on Transco, an institutional permitting capability that the Zamarin transition did not impair, and marginal Transco build multiples that have improved to sub-4x because hyperscaler counterparties are price-agnostic and supply is scarce. That is a regulated-utility-plus-industrial-compounder return profile, not a midstream return profile. Closing that gap is what's left.

The price problem is real. WMB trades at roughly 12x 2026E and 11x 2027E EV/EBITDA — above its 5-year average of 10.8x and within range of the sell-side targets. Leverage rises into the 4.0–4.4x band through 2027 against management's 3.5–4.0x guide. The asymmetric entry was twelve to eighteen months ago. We are buying durability, not dislocation.

**Rating: Buy.** I would position this as a durable core holding rather than a max-conviction overweight. Conditions to upsize: (i) a pullback toward $65 on capex-execution stumbles, NESE delays, or transient leverage anxiety; (ii) confirmed PI counterparty diversification on NEO or Project #5/#6; or (iii) federal permitting reform that advantages established incumbents. Conditions to downgrade map to the thesis kill list in the Conclusion.

## Our Thesis

I believe the market is mispricing the durability of Transco's competitive advantage period at 10–15 years when the right number is 20–25+ years. That CAP gap is the source of the remaining alpha after the BTM re-rating. Power Innovation is correctly identified by consensus as transformational; what consensus underprices is that the integrated stack — Williams owning gas supply, transportation, and the turbine — structurally insulates the franchise from the heat-rate critique that has driven IPPs away from simple-cycle behind-the-meter.

## Proof Points

### Transco passes the Fork Test as an unforkable Atlantic corridor

Two independent expert sources — including the most skeptical voice we engaged — deliver near-textbook Tier 6 descriptions. A consulting manager at East Daley Analytics, modeling Williams at the asset level with no corporate bias, characterizes Transco as "effectively does have a monopoly over that Atlantic region." A former Williams business development lead with Gulf Coast pipeline experience — the skeptical voice across most of the practitioner record — independently confirms that in the corridor east of the Appalachian Mountains, replicating the infrastructure is "very difficult" and that a data-center developer requiring quarter- to half-BCF could "not justify building back to a production basin." When the skeptic delivers a textbook Tier 6 description, the moat finding is corroborated rather than asserted.

### Permitting capability is institutional, not personal — and survives the Zamarin transition

The single biggest analytical risk going in was that Williams' execution moat travels with Alan Armstrong and decays under Chad Zamarin. Two senior former Williams sources — a former Director of Energy Policy and Regulatory Affairs (June 2025, post-Zamarin) and a former SVP of Operations — independently describe the capability as process-driven, formalized, and "top of class." The Director characterizes Williams as "really good at large, long, capital projects" with proactive community engagement; the SVP describes a "rigorous, formalized annual process" for capital and project prioritization. The chaotic-management narrative anchored in 2020–2023 is real but pre-Zamarin succession politics, not capability erosion. This is the most consequential finding because it converts a novel claim — institutional permitting as a Catalini moat the sell-side does not credit — into a corroborated one.

### Marginal Transco build multiples are improving, not compressing

The bear interpretation of Transco's high contracted utilization is that Williams is out of high-return expansion opportunities and Power Innovation is the bailout. The East Daley evidence cuts the other way. Southeast Supply Enhancement and Power Express are landing at sub-4x EBITDA build multiples — meaningfully better than the 4–6x mid-range Williams achieved historically. The mechanism is supply scarcity meeting price-agnostic hyperscaler demand: "data centers are relatively price agnostic, driving highly lucrative expansion projects with cost-to-EBITDA multiples sitting at or below 5.0." The trajectory is more capital-intensive — twinning lines rather than compression upgrades — but the returns on that capital are higher, not lower.

### The Power Innovation moat survives the "IPPs avoid simple-cycle" critique

The most damaging signal in the practitioner record is that competing IPPs (Vistra, Talen, NRG, NextEra) are deliberately avoiding the simple-cycle BTM strategy Williams is pursuing. The screen reads that as a moat-eroding signal; the underlying evidence does not support that reading. IPPs are avoiding simple-cycle for sound structural reasons — heat-rate inefficiency at baseload, post-Talen/AWS FERC backlash on BTM carve-outs, and capital discipline from boards scarred by prior overbuild. None of those reasons binds Williams. Williams' fixed PPA structure passes gas cost to the customer, insulating the franchise from the thermodynamic inefficiency. Williams is a midstream developer with an integrated stack, not an IPP selling into wholesale markets, so the regulatory architecture is different. And Williams owns the gas, the pipe, and the turbine — capturing the full vertical including the gas transportation margin an IPP would have to pay Williams to obtain. The 12-month combined-cycle backlog (vs. 5–7 years for SC turbines) is a binding hyperscaler constraint, and Williams uniquely satisfies it.

### The CAP duration mispricing is the largest single source of remaining alpha

Consensus models Transco's competitive advantage period at roughly 10–15 years. The Tier 6 coordination equilibrium described by both independent practitioner sources, combined with Williams' institutional permitting capability, supports 20–25+ years. The Atlantic Sunrise project required thirteen years of litigation to build; replicating Transco's right-of-way today "would take 20+ years and likely fail in court." A CAP extension of five to ten years on a Tier 6 asset is materially value-creative — the duration of moat-protected cash flow, not the absolute level, is what the market discounts most aggressively, and it is the dimension where consensus is most anchored on midstream-cycle base rates rather than utility-cycle base rates.

### Composite re-pricing toward the utility / industrial compounder comp set

By 2030, more than 60% of EBITDA is expected to come from long-term take-or-pay contracts and Power Innovation reaches roughly 30% of consolidated EBITDA from 0% today. The earnings algorithm — contracted, regulated, take-or-pay — converges toward a regulated utility or industrial compounder, not a midstream operator. WMB screens cheap on P/E relative to utilities and cheap versus midstream peers on EV/EBITDA against the growth profile. Jefferies has begun referencing PEG-like (EV/G) and industrial-compounder comps (Ecolab, Linde, Waste Management) — the standard sell-side mechanism when the conventional multiple is stretched but the cash-flow profile says it shouldn't be. I would weight the utility comp more than the industrial comp; Power Innovation's contracted cash flow is the closer analog.

## The Business

### How it makes money

Williams operates four reporting segments. Transmission is the franchise — Transco at its core, the largest interstate natural gas pipeline in the U.S., delivering roughly one-third of U.S. natural gas demand. Transmission contributes roughly 48% of EBITDA today, falling to ~40% by 2030 only because the segment denominator grows with Power Innovation, not because Transmission shrinks. Gathering & Processing (Northeast G&P plus West) is the second pillar at ~45% of EBITDA, anchored in the Marcellus, Haynesville, and Permian and increasingly important as the gas supply tether for both LNG export and behind-the-meter power. Gas & NGL Marketing is small (~7%) and earnings-volatile; the Sequent franchise inside it is, on the practitioner evidence, more commoditized than IR framing suggests. Power Innovation is the new fourth segment — six announced behind-the-meter and turnkey projects totaling ~$9.65B of committed capex, ~2.6 GW announced (~1.9 GW excluding NEO), and roughly $1.93B of EBITDA by 2029.

### Competitive position and moat

Williams' moat is a three-layer reinforcing chain. The base layer is Transco's coordination equilibrium — FERC certificates, right-of-way, state and local permits, decades of community relationships, and federal preemption of state 401 vetoes. A competitor with infinite capital cannot duplicate the corridor; the right-of-way alone takes 20+ years and frequently fails in court. The middle layer is institutional permitting capability, a process-driven organizational muscle for navigating multi-jurisdictional approvals on large capital projects. The top layer is multi-party coordination switching costs on firm transportation contracts — ~95% contracted, take-or-pay, with regulatory dependencies that make migration prohibitive for shippers. The Catalini screen places this composite in the FORTRESS quadrant with classical moat strength of 4.0–4.2 and agentic vulnerability of 1.7–2.0. Williams' value sits in atoms and political relationships, not bits that AI agents can replicate or disintermediate.

Power Innovation sits in the EMERGING quadrant — a transitioning capability rather than a fortified moat. The current advantage is real: speed-to-market via reserved turbines, integrated gas supply through Williams' existing gathering footprint, and the ability to act as a turnkey EPC provider rather than charging a midstream toll plus a separate IPP margin. Heat-rate economics are a real long-term risk past initial 10–12.5 year contract expiration (2036–2038). Through the contract period, the fixed PPA structure passes gas cost to the customer; after it, the moat depends on combined-cycle conversion economics, contract renewal at acceptable terms, or grid integration. That is a long-dated risk worth monitoring rather than discounting now.

### Growth vectors

Transmission shadow backlog of roughly $15.5B in unsanctioned expansion opportunities — Jefferies translates this into ~$2.5B of incremental EBITDA capacity. Power Innovation backlog of 6 GW of unsanctioned projects with reserved equipment, totaling ~$23B of potential capex and ~$4.6B of EBITDA by 2032 at the current build multiple. Coal-to-gas conversions across the Southeast as data-center load drives reliability needs. LNG export connectivity at the Gulf Coast. The growth is large enough that even haircutting hit rates and build multiples leaves the franchise compounding.

### Management and capital allocation

Chad Zamarin became CEO in July 2025, succeeding Alan Armstrong. The practitioner record on Zamarin is mixed-to-positive — characterized by one source as "brand-focused, not necessarily an expert on the fundamentals," but with explicit credit for leveraging the brand-focused approach to advance regulatory goodwill via net zero pledges, which has strategic value in the permitting context. The institutional capability is process-driven and survives the leadership transition, per multiple senior former Williams sources. One yellow flag from the screen: compensation does not specifically reward contract duration extension or net retention. A management team could deploy capital at attractive 5x multiples with concentrated counterparty exposure and shorter contract durations and still hit comp targets. The 12.5-year terms on Apollo, Aquila, and NEO are positive but shorter than the 15–20 year tenors management is socializing — and meaningfully shorter than asset useful life. Compensation alignment is a watch item, not a thesis breaker.

## What Could Go Wrong

1. **Power Innovation cost overruns or single-counterparty stress.** Four of five PI projects appear to anchor on Meta per the 10-K and Sidecat regulatory filings. Cost overruns above 15% on Socrates or NEO impair the 5x build multiple; a Meta-side budget pullback or contract renegotiation would compress the franchise's perceived durability and reverse a meaningful share of the YTD re-rating. I am monitoring NEO and the next two FIDs for non-Meta hyperscaler anchors.

2. **Leverage above guide for longer than communicated.** Jefferies models 4.41x peak Net Debt/EBITDA in 2027, RBC has it at 3.95x in 2026, UBS at 4.1x. The right number depends on how aggressively Williams FIDs the unsanctioned PI backlog. Sustained leverage above 4.5x past 2028 would force equity issuance or distribution-growth deceleration; this is a discrete thesis kill condition, not just a watch item.

3. **Heat-rate economics and contract renewal post-2036–2038.** The thermodynamic critique of simple-cycle as baseload is real; Williams' fixed-PPA structure insulates through initial contract terms but not beyond. After expiration the moat depends on combined-cycle conversion economics, contract renewal at acceptable terms, or grid integration. This is a long-dated risk that does not affect near-term scoring but should be monitored as combined-cycle wait-lists shorten and grid integration capacity expands.

4. **SCOTUS unwinding federal preemption on state 401 permits.** Federal preemption is the load-bearing element of the institutional permitting moat. A SCOTUS decision restoring meaningful state-level veto power on interstate pipelines would convert Williams' permitting capability from a defensible moat into a chronic execution risk. I view the probability as low but the magnitude as severe — it is the most consequential tail risk in the thesis.

## Conclusion

**Rating: Buy**, sized as a durable core holding rather than a max-conviction overweight. The re-rate is not finished, but the remaining alpha is structural — the market still prices WMB as a midstream operator when the right comp set is regulated utility plus industrial compounder, and the CAP-duration mispricing is the load-bearing variable. We can revisit sizing on a pullback toward $65 or on a confirmed PI counterparty diversification event.

Next decision points: Q2 2026 earnings (counterparty diversification on PI; build-multiple update on SSE and Power Express; leverage trajectory); NESE in-service milestone; any of the kill conditions — SCOTUS overturning federal preemption on 401 permits, Transco rate case producing material rate reduction, hyperscaler BTM demand collapse, catastrophic operational failure on a flagship project, cost overruns above 15% on Socrates or NEO, or sustained leverage above 4.5x past 2028.
