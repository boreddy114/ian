## Summary

The debate is not whether the market missed data-center power demand. That theme is visible and the stock has rerated from roughly $52 to $72 over the last twelve months on the back of it. Sell-side now sits at $81–$91 — RBC $82, Jefferies $81, UBS $91 — implying 13–26% upside. The question that brings us here is whether AI load growth changes the duration of Williams' excess-return runway, especially on Transco.

I believe it does, and that the change is not fully priced. The market reads Williams as a premium midstream operator with a valuable behind-the-meter call option. The evidence supports something different: a Tier 6 coordination equilibrium with a 20–25+ year competitive advantage period on Transco, an institutional permitting capability that the Zamarin transition did not impair, and marginal Transco build multiples that have improved to sub-4x because hyperscaler counterparties are price-agnostic and corridor capacity is scarce. Power Innovation is the monetization mechanism, not the moat. The durable asset is Transco's scarce corridor and integrated gas-delivery architecture. PI matters because it demonstrates what that corridor is worth under a new demand regime — and that demonstration is the remaining alpha.

## Our Thesis

I believe the market is mispricing the durability of Transco's competitive advantage period at 10–15 years when the right number is 20–25+ years; closing that gap is the source of the remaining alpha after the BTM re-rating. Power Innovation is the monetization mechanism, not the moat — its value is that it demonstrates what Transco's corridor is worth under a new demand regime, structurally insulated by the integrated stack (gas supply, transportation, turbines) and a fixed-PPA contract structure that passes thermodynamic risk to the customer.

## Proof Points

### Transco passes the Fork Test as an unforkable Atlantic corridor

Two independent expert sources — including the most skeptical voice we engaged — deliver near-textbook Tier 6 descriptions. A consulting manager at East Daley Analytics, modeling Williams at the asset level with no corporate bias, characterizes Transco as "effectively does have a monopoly over that Atlantic region." A former Williams business development lead with Gulf Coast pipeline experience — the skeptical voice across most of the practitioner record — independently confirms that in the corridor east of the Appalachian Mountains, replicating the infrastructure is "very difficult" and that a data-center developer requiring quarter- to half-BCF could "not justify building back to a production basin." When the skeptic delivers a textbook Tier 6 description, the moat finding is corroborated rather than asserted.

### Permitting capability is institutional, not personal — and survives the Zamarin transition

The biggest diligence risk going in was that Williams' execution moat travels with Alan Armstrong and decays under Chad Zamarin. Two senior former Williams sources — a former Director of Energy Policy and Regulatory Affairs (June 2025, post-Zamarin) and a former SVP of Operations — independently describe the capability as process-driven, formalized, and "top of class." The Director characterizes Williams as "really good at large, long, capital projects" with proactive community engagement; the SVP describes a "rigorous, formalized annual process" for capital and project prioritization. The chaotic-management narrative anchored in the 2020–2023 succession period is pre-Zamarin politics, not capability erosion. This converts a novel claim — institutional permitting as a moat the sell-side does not credit — into a corroborated one.

### Scarcity is improving marginal economics, not exhausting the runway

The intuitive bear case is that high utilization means Transco is out of high-return expansions. The evidence cuts the other way. Southeast Supply Enhancement and Power Express are landing at sub-4x EBITDA build multiples — meaningfully better than the 4–6x mid-range Williams achieved historically. The East Daley source describes the mechanism directly: "data centers are relatively price agnostic, driving highly lucrative expansion projects with cost-to-EBITDA multiples sitting at or below 5.0." The trajectory is more capital-intensive — twinning lines rather than compression upgrades — but the returns on that capital are higher, not lower. This is the most observable test of the duration thesis: if marginal multiples stay sub-4x, the thesis strengthens; if they drift back to 5–6x without offsetting volume or tenor benefits, it weakens.

### Power Innovation is the monetization mechanism — and the IPP critique does not bind

The most damaging signal in the practitioner record is that competing IPPs (Vistra, Talen, NRG, NextEra) are deliberately avoiding the simple-cycle BTM strategy Williams is pursuing. The screen initially read this as a moat-eroding signal; the underlying evidence does not support that reading. IPPs avoid simple-cycle for sound structural reasons — heat-rate inefficiency at baseload, post-Talen/AWS FERC backlash on BTM carve-outs, and capital discipline from boards scarred by prior overbuild. None binds Williams. The fixed PPA structure passes gas cost to the customer, insulating the franchise from the thermodynamic inefficiency. Williams is a midstream developer with an integrated stack, not an IPP selling into wholesale markets, so the regulatory architecture is different. And Williams owns the gas, the pipe, and the turbine — capturing the full vertical including the gas-transportation margin an IPP would have to pay Williams to obtain. The 12-month combined-cycle backlog versus 5–7 years for simple-cycle turbines is a binding hyperscaler constraint, and Williams uniquely satisfies it during the initial contract period.

### The CAP duration mispricing is the largest single source of remaining alpha

Consensus models Transco's competitive advantage period at roughly 10–15 years. The Tier 6 coordination equilibrium described by both independent practitioner sources, combined with Williams' institutional permitting capability, supports 20–25+ years. The Atlantic Sunrise project required thirteen years of litigation to build; replicating Transco's right-of-way today "would take 20+ years and likely fail in court." A CAP extension of five to ten years on a Tier 6 asset is materially value-creative — the duration of moat-protected cash flow, not the absolute level, is the dimension where consensus is most anchored on midstream-cycle base rates rather than utility-cycle base rates.

## The Business

### How it makes money

Williams operates four economic pillars. Transmission is the franchise — Transco at its core, the largest interstate natural gas pipeline in the U.S., delivering roughly one-third of U.S. natural gas demand. Transmission contributes roughly 48% of EBITDA today, falling to ~40% by 2030 only because the segment denominator grows with Power Innovation, not because Transmission shrinks. Gathering & Processing (Northeast G&P plus West) is the second pillar at ~45% of EBITDA, anchored in the Marcellus, Haynesville, and Permian and increasingly important as the gas supply tether for both LNG export and behind-the-meter power. Gas & NGL Marketing is small (~7%) and earnings-volatile; the Sequent franchise inside it is, on the practitioner evidence, more commoditized than IR framing suggests. Power Innovation is the new fourth segment — six announced behind-the-meter and turnkey projects totaling ~$9.65B of committed capex, ~2.6 GW announced, and roughly $1.93B of EBITDA by 2029.

### Competitive position and moat

Williams' moat is a three-layer reinforcing chain. The base layer is Transco's coordination equilibrium — FERC certificates, right-of-way, state and local permits, decades of community relationships, and federal preemption of state 401 vetoes. A competitor with infinite capital cannot duplicate the corridor; the right-of-way alone takes 20+ years and frequently fails in court. The middle layer is institutional permitting capability, a process-driven organizational muscle for navigating multi-jurisdictional approvals on large capital projects. The top layer is multi-party coordination switching costs on firm transportation contracts — ~95% contracted, take-or-pay, with regulatory dependencies that make migration prohibitive for shippers. The composite places Williams in the FORTRESS quadrant of the Catalini framework. The value sits in atoms and political relationships, not bits that AI agents can replicate or disintermediate.

Power Innovation sits in the EMERGING quadrant — a transitioning capability rather than a fortified moat. The current advantage is real: speed-to-market via reserved turbines, integrated gas supply through Williams' existing gathering footprint, and the ability to act as a turnkey EPC provider rather than charging a midstream toll plus a separate IPP margin. Heat-rate economics are a real long-term risk past initial 10–12.5 year contract expiration (2036–2038). Through the contract period, the fixed PPA structure passes gas cost to the customer; after it, the moat depends on combined-cycle conversion economics, contract renewal at acceptable terms, or grid integration. This is a long-dated risk worth monitoring, not discounting now.

### Growth vectors

Transmission shadow backlog of roughly $15.5B in unsanctioned expansion opportunities — Jefferies translates this into ~$2.5B of incremental EBITDA capacity. Power Innovation backlog of 6 GW of unsanctioned projects with reserved equipment, totaling ~$23B of potential capex and ~$4.6B of EBITDA by 2032 at the current build multiple. Coal-to-gas conversions across the Southeast as data-center load drives reliability needs. LNG export connectivity at the Gulf Coast. The growth is large enough that even haircutting hit rates and build multiples leaves the franchise compounding.

### Management and capital allocation

Chad Zamarin became CEO in July 2025, succeeding Alan Armstrong. The practitioner record on Zamarin is mixed-to-positive — characterized by one source as "brand-focused, not necessarily an expert on the fundamentals," with explicit credit for leveraging that brand-focused approach to advance regulatory goodwill via net zero pledges, which has strategic value in the permitting context. The institutional capability is process-driven and survives the leadership transition per multiple senior former Williams sources. One yellow flag: compensation does not specifically reward contract duration extension or net retention. A management team could deploy capital at attractive 5x multiples with concentrated counterparty exposure and shorter contract durations and still hit comp targets. The 12.5-year terms on Apollo, Aquila, and NEO are positive but shorter than the 15–20 year tenors management is socializing and meaningfully shorter than asset useful life. Compensation alignment is a watch item, not a thesis breaker.

The case rests on duration. If Transco is a 10–15 year moat, the stock is probably close to fair value after the rerating. If Transco is a 20–25+ year moat with recurring sub-4x expansion opportunities tied to power scarcity, the appropriate framework shifts toward contracted infrastructure compounding. The same EBITDA stream is worth more because the protected reinvestment runway is longer. The cleanest way to express the upside is through CAP extension rather than a heroic multiple — extending the period of protected, high-return cash flows from 10–15 to 20–25+ years generates roughly 30–40% of present-value uplift under standard mid-cycle assumptions, before any multiple-rerating mechanic.

## What Could Go Wrong

1. **Power Innovation cost overruns or single-counterparty stress.** Four of five PI projects appear to anchor on Meta per the 10-K and Sidecat regulatory filings. Cost overruns above 15% on Socrates or NEO impair the 5x build multiple; a Meta-side budget pullback or contract renegotiation would compress the franchise's perceived durability and reverse a meaningful share of the YTD re-rating. NEO and the next two FIDs are the discrete tests for non-Meta hyperscaler anchors.

2. **Leverage above guide for longer than communicated.** Jefferies models 4.41x peak Net Debt/EBITDA in 2027, RBC has it at 3.95x in 2026, UBS at 4.1x. The right number depends on how aggressively Williams FIDs the unsanctioned PI backlog. Sustained leverage above 4.5x past 2028 would force equity issuance or distribution-growth deceleration; this is a discrete thesis kill condition, not a watch item.

3. **Marginal Transco build multiples drift back to historical 4–6x without offsetting volume or duration benefits.** This is the most observable test of the duration thesis — if scarcity stops improving reinvestment economics, the bull case loses its load-bearing input and the comp set defaults back to midstream rather than contracted infrastructure compounder.

4. **Heat-rate economics and contract renewal post-2036–2038.** The thermodynamic critique of simple-cycle as baseload is real; the fixed-PPA structure insulates through initial contract terms but not beyond. After expiration the moat depends on combined-cycle conversion economics, renewal at acceptable terms, or grid integration. Long-dated, but the framing of PI as durable vs. transient hinges on this.

5. **SCOTUS unwinding federal preemption on state 401 permits.** Federal preemption is the load-bearing element of the institutional permitting moat. A SCOTUS decision restoring meaningful state-level veto power on interstate pipelines would convert Williams' permitting capability from a defensible moat into a chronic execution risk. Probability low, magnitude severe — the most consequential tail risk in the thesis.
