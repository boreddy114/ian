# Investment Philosophy — Reference Document

This is a condensed reference for the analytical framework governing all investment
analysis. It is derived from a longer working philosophy document and from accumulated
research practice.

---

## First Principles

**Stocks are ownership interests in real businesses.** A stock is a fractional claim on
future cash flows. This determines the time horizon (long), the analytical focus
(business fundamentals over price action), and the emotional posture (patience over
anxiety). Daily price fluctuation is informational, not directional.

**Value is the net present value of future free cash flow.** Every valuation method —
multiples, SOTP, replacement cost — is a shortcut to or derivative of a DCF. We always
think in terms of cash generation, reinvestment opportunity, and the duration and
reliability of those cash flows. Cash economics drive long-term shareholder returns,
not accounting earnings.

**Risk is the probability of permanent capital impairment.** Not volatility. The three
mechanisms for permanent impairment: overpayment (buying above intrinsic value with no
margin of safety), catastrophic dilution (equity issuance at distressed prices or for
value-destroying acquisitions), and inattention to whether cash flow is truly
distributable to owners. This means we spend more time on downside analysis than upside
scenarios.

**Incentives matter.** Alignment between management and shareholders is one of the most
reliable predictors of long-term capital allocation quality. Options create convex payoffs
that reward volatility. Ownership creates linear payoffs that reward value creation.
Look for the latter.

**The market is informationally efficient but not always correct.** Prices reflect
available information, but the consensus interpretation can be wrong. The gap between
price and value is the raw material for outperformance. It emerges from interpreting
common information more clearly and acting on it more patiently.

## Where Excess Returns Come From

**Analytical advantage** is the primary edge. It does not require knowing something the
market does not. It requires working with known data and arranging and weighing the
information in a non-consensus way through brainpower, organization, and logic. Accounting
fluency is a prerequisite. The Four M's framework is the primary structuring tool. We
invest within a circle of competence and draw from a continuously expanding business model
inventory.

**Behavioral advantage** means having the structural and psychological ability to act
rationally when the market is not. Understanding decision-making pitfalls: anchoring,
confirmation bias, loss aversion, recency bias, narrative fallacy. Descriptions can
corrupt thought — how a situation is framed often determines how it is judged.

**Informational advantage** is rare and fleeting. We do not build the process around it.
Good due diligence confirms what is knowable; an informational edge reveals what is not
yet known. When it exists, we use it.

**Opportunity set matters.** Not all ponds are equally well-stocked. Edge is not portable.
Look for higher dispersion, less coverage, and wider gaps between price and value.

## The Four M's

### Moat

A structural feature that allows returns on invested capital above cost of capital for an
extended period. Evaluate: barriers to entry, market structure, competitive/technological/
regulatory risks, unlevered returns on capital, supplier/distributor dynamics, value chain
economics.

Reinvestment is equally important. A wide moat with no reinvestment opportunities is a cash
cow, not a compounder. Distinguish maintenance from growth capital expenditure — this is one
of the most underappreciated dimensions of fundamental analysis.

### Management

Capital allocation is the most important job of senior management. Ask whether management
will allocate capital to maximize shareholder returns — or something else. Evaluate:
incentive structure, M&A track record, treatment of surplus capital, alignment with
long-term owners.

### Margin of Safety

Not a single number but a way of thinking that confronts the probability and magnitude of
permanent loss before committing capital. Evaluate: revenue/profit diversification,
financial strength, self-funding capacity, competitor financial strength, funding
sustainability, liability duration. And the simple observation: pay less than what
something is worth.

### Mr. Market

The market is a counterparty, not an oracle. Ask: would we want to own the entire company
in illiquid form? What is the sustainable cash-on-cash yield? Where do Mr. Market's biases
lie — and where do our own? Position sizing must account for conviction level and the risk
of being wrong.

## Mental Models and Business Model Inventory

The goal is to internalize frameworks, not to label analysis with them. When we see a new
company, we want to recognize the pattern: platform, razor-and-blade, capital-light
compounder with switching costs, aggregator, physical-tether utility.

**Active mental models:**

- **Porter's Five Forces** — industry structure and competitive dynamics
- **Aggregation Theory** (Ben Thompson) — digital platforms that own consumer relationships
  and commoditize supply
- **Capital cycle theory** (Marathon/Chancellor) — supply-side dynamics driving long-term
  returns
- **Jobs-to-be-done** (Christensen) — demand-side competitive analysis
- **Commoditizing complements** — making adjacent products cheaper to increase core demand
- **Mauboussin EVA / ROIC-WACC** — value creation decomposition, multiple compression as
  arithmetic identity
- **Slywotzky profit pool migration** — where value creation is headed, not where it's been
- **Network effects** — type, strength, defensibility
- **Replacement cost** — what it would cost to recreate the asset base
- **Robustness ratio** — resilience under stress
- **Latent pricing power** — unexercised ability to raise prices

**Current regime lens: Scarcity**

The investable universe is organized into three zones:

- **Zone 1 — Physical Bottleneck Scarcity:** Companies with irreplaceable physical assets
  where AI is additive, not substitutive. Require physical tethers — assets that cannot be
  replicated by software or AI. This is where durable value concentrates.
- **Zone 2 — Straddlers:** Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft. Massive scale, both physical and
  digital. Evaluate on capital allocation and ROIC trajectory.
- **Zone 3 — Abundance / Software Compression:** Software companies in the compression zone
  where AI substitutes for the product itself. The payments industry precedent is the
  governing analogy — entire sectors compress to 6-8x regardless of proposed moats.

The Spectrum Framework categorizes software companies from Pure App (Compression Zone) to
Pure Infra (Utility Duration) using a 5-question sorting methodology.

Scarcity is the current regime lens, not the sole one. The frameworks above are
complementary and the right one surfaces based on the specific business under analysis.

## Process Discipline

- Consistently apply the Four M's to every investment decision
- Document reasoning before making a decision (distinguish skill from luck)
- Conduct pre-mortems (what would have to be true for this to fail?)
- Conduct post-mortems on successes and failures
- Be honest about what we do not know
- Guard against the gradual erosion of discipline — the drift that happens one shortcut
  at a time

## Source Hierarchy and Data Rules

### Acceptable Sources (in priority order)

1. **User-provided documents** — anything uploaded to the conversation or available in a
   Project knowledge base. These are the highest-priority source. Read all of them before
   writing anything.
2. **SEC filings** — 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, 20-F, proxy statements. Fetch directly from
   SEC EDGAR or the company's IR page. These are the authoritative source for GAAP
   financials, SBC reconciliation, segment data, and risk factors.
3. **Company IR materials** — earnings releases, shareholder letters, investor
   presentations, supplemental data packages. Fetch from the company's investor relations
   website directly.
4. **Earnings call transcripts** — for management language analysis, tone shifts, and
   Q&A dynamics. Use for qualitative insight, not as the primary source for financial facts.
5. **FDIC Call Reports, ISA filings, permits, satellite data** — for primary verification
   of specific claims (bank subsidiary financials, infrastructure projects, etc.).
6. **Expert network calls and practitioner interviews** — when provided by the user.
7. **Sell-side research** — for consensus positioning and variant perception only.
   Never adopt sell-side conclusions as our own.

### Explicitly Blocked Sources

Never use any of the following as sources for facts, analysis, or investment conclusions:

- **Motley Fool** — retail-oriented, no analytical value
- **Seeking Alpha** — user-generated content, unreliable
- **Yahoo Finance articles** — aggregated news, not primary
- **TipRanks, MarketBeat, GuruFocus** — retail screening tools, not research
- **CoinCodex, StockAnalysis, WallStreetZen** — price prediction sites
- **Any AI-generated summary or news recap** — derivative, not primary
- **News articles, blogs, or opinion pieces** — never as evidence for investment conclusions

If a web search returns only retail or aggregator sources for a data point, do not use
them. Instead, state that the primary source was not available and flag the gap.

MacroTrends is acceptable only for quick historical data lookups (revenue history, SBC
history) where the underlying data traces to SEC filings — but always verify critical
numbers against the actual filing when possible.

### Data Retrieval Process

Before writing any investment document:

1. **Read all user-provided documents first** — research folders, prior memos, uploaded
   filings, knowledge base contents. This is the foundation.
2. **Fetch the actual earnings release** from the company IR page or SEC EDGAR — not a
   news article about the earnings.
3. **Fetch the 10-K or 10-Q** for GAAP reconciliation tables — especially SBC expense,
   segment-level detail, and cash flow statements.
4. **State which documents were used** in the analysis and explicitly flag any gaps where
   primary sources were unavailable.
5. **If working without a knowledge base**, say so prominently. Analysis depth will be
   limited and the document should reflect that honestly.

## EquityVault — Research Directory Structure

When pointed at the EquityVault research directory (or any structured research folder),
navigate it deliberately. The structure is:

```
EquityVault/
├── 30_Companies/{TICKER}/
│   ├── Thesis_Log.md        ← Continuity layer. Read first. Our current view.
│   ├── IC_Memo.md           ← Formal IC memo if exists.
│   ├── ACTIVE_{title}.docx  ← Current distributable thesis. Only one per folder.
│   ├── [first-party notes]  ← Our own analysis. Highest analytical weight.
│   ├── 3P_Research/         ← Sell-side, expert calls. For context, not conclusions.
│   └── Outputs/             ← Previous versions, superseded documents.
├── 40_Themes/               ← Framework docs: scarcity, spectrum, capital cycle, etc.
└── 50_Research_Notes/        ← Cross-cutting sector and macro research.
```

**The ACTIVE_ convention:** Only one file per company folder carries the `ACTIVE_` prefix.
This is the current distributable thesis document. The title after `ACTIVE_` is meaningful
(e.g., `ACTIVE_NFLX_Winning_Aggregator_in_AI_Era.docx`). When superseded, the old
document loses the prefix and moves to Outputs/.

**For known companies:** Read Thesis_Log.md first, then the ACTIVE_ document, then
first-party notes, then reference 3P_Research for consensus. Run the Making Money Models
checklist against the company — identify which of the 37 frameworks fit, which conflict,
and where the market may be mispricing. Go to 40_Themes/ to map to relevant themes. Check
50_Research_Notes/ for sector context.

**For new companies:** Read whatever is in the folder, then run the Making Money Models
checklist. Go to 40_Themes/ and pattern-match: which frameworks apply? Surface both the
fit and the tensions explicitly — the best analysis happens when frameworks collide.
Flag what first-party analysis is missing.

**Weight hierarchy:** First-party analysis > SEC filings > company IR > 3P research.
Our own work always carries the highest weight. Sell-side is for positioning, not
conclusions.
