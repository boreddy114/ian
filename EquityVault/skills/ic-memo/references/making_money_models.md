# Making Money Models: Investment Frameworks Reference

> **Purpose:** This document is a decision-forcing framework for equity research. When analyzing a new company or security, systematically scan each framework below and **actively challenge the investment thesis** by reporting which patterns fit, which patterns conflict, and which lenses might be missing.
>
> **How to use this document:** Do not merely confirm the thesis. When a new security is presented, scan all frameworks and *proactively flag tensions and contradictions.* If a company looks like a capital cycle opportunity but sits in an abundance layer vulnerable to disruption, say so. If a stock looks cheap on replacement cost but has no physical tether in a scarcity regime, say so. The goal is rigorous debate: identify *why* a security might be mispriced, *what kind* of opportunity it represents, and *what frameworks argue against the position.* The best investment discussions happen when frameworks collide.

---

## I. CAPITAL CYCLE & SUPPLY-SIDE FRAMEWORKS

### 1. The Capital Cycle
**Source:** Marathon Asset Management, Edward Chancellor, Omar Malik / Hosking Partners
High returns attract capital → new supply → returns fall → capital exits → supply contracts → returns recover. Supply dynamics are more predictable than demand but systematically under-analyzed. Ask: Where is this industry in the capital cycle? Is capital entering or leaving?

### 2. Supply Side Dominates Demand Side
**Source:** Marathon, Omar Malik
Strong demand growth is neither necessary nor sufficient for strong shareholder returns. A favorable supply side is both necessary and sufficient. Tobacco: volumes halved, outperformed by 400%+. Airlines: stellar demand, terrible returns. Ask: Could this company earn attractive returns even if demand disappoints?

### 3. Industry Consolidation as Buy Signal
**Source:** Marathon, Omar Malik
The moment an industry transitions from fragmented competition to oligopoly is the key inflection. Prices are cheap, investors uninterested, insiders doubling down. Ask: Is this industry mid-consolidation? What will it look like with 3-4 players controlling 80%+?

### 4. ROIC Trajectory Over Earnings Game
**Source:** Omar Malik, Edward Chancellor
Over the long run, return on capital — not quarterly earnings — determines share price direction. Ask: Is the ROIC trend improving due to structural supply-side dynamics? Is the quarterly noise masking the signal?

### 5. QE and Cheap Money as Capital Cycle Distortion
**Source:** Marathon Asset Management
Cheap money keeps zombies alive, preventing supply-side cleanup. Patient investors suffer extended troughs but benefit from more violent resolution. Ask: Are zombie competitors being kept alive? What happens when they exit?

## II. COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE & MOAT ASSESSMENT

### 6. ROIC Spread + Competitive Advantage Period / "Beat the Fade"
**Source:** Michael Mauboussin
The market prices an implied fade rate. If you can assess whether fade is slower or faster than expected, you've found a mispricing. Ask: What fade rate is the market pricing? Based on moat analysis, will fade be faster or slower?

### 7. Five Forces as Moat Measurement Tool
**Source:** Mauboussin via Porter
Systematic force-by-force analysis: threat of entry, buyer power, supplier power, substitutes, rivalry. Ask: Which force is strongest/weakest? Where is the market misjudging?

### 8. Economies of Scale: MES vs. TAM
**Source:** Mauboussin
Calculate minimum efficient scale relative to TAM. This tells you how many profitable competitors the market can structurally support. Ask: How many profitable competitors can this industry support?

### 9. Network Effects Taxonomy
**Source:** Mauboussin
Not all network effects are equal. Direct vs. indirect, local vs. global. Many claimed network effects are weak. Ask: What type? How strong? Is the market over- or under-crediting?

### 10. Switching Costs Analysis
**Source:** Mauboussin
Procedural (time/hassle), financial (direct cost), relational (emotional/identity). Multiple types compound. Ask: What types exist? Are they increasing or decreasing?

### 11. Brand vs. Commodity — Real Pricing Power Test
**Source:** Mauboussin
Genuine brand value only exists when it allows a meaningful premium above commodity price. Many famous brands have no actual pricing power. Ask: Does this brand support real pricing power, or just awareness?

### 12. Disruption / Innovator's Dilemma
**Source:** Mauboussin via Christensen
Sustaining innovations help incumbents; disruptive innovations destroy them. Key: disruptive innovations start in overlooked segments. Ask: Is this genuine disruption or a sustaining innovation? Is the market over- or under-estimating the threat?

### 13. Game Theory / Competitive Dynamics
**Source:** Mauboussin
Prisoner's dilemma, tit-for-tat signaling, Colonel Blotto. Predicting shifts in competitive behavior creates alpha. Ask: What game are competitors playing? Is behavior about to shift?

### 14. Scale Economies Shared — The Flywheel Model
**Source:** Nomad Investment Partnership (Nick Sleep)
Companies that share scale benefits with customers through lower prices. Flywheel: lower prices → more customers → more scale → even lower costs. Market sees "margin pressure," reality is "compounding competitive advantage." Ask: Is this company sharing or extracting? Is the flywheel spinning?

## III. VALUATION FRAMEWORKS & MISPRICING MECHANISMS

### 15. Discount to Replacement Cost + Latent Pricing Power
**Source:** Nomad (Sleep)
Buy assets below what it costs to rebuild them, especially with untapped pricing power. Combination provides margin of safety plus upside catalyst. Ask: What would it cost to replicate this asset base? Is market cap below that?

### 16. Replacement Value as Capital Cycle Engine
**Source:** Omar Malik
When valued above replacement cost → new supply incentivized. When below → no new supply arrives. Ask: Is the valuation incentivizing or deterring new supply?

### 17. The Description Problem / Support Theory
**Source:** Bill Miller
Probabilities attach to descriptions of events, not events themselves. The market's prevailing description may be a legacy frame that no longer fits. Ask: What is the market's description? Is it accurate? Would a more accurate description change the valuation?

### 18. "Looking Expensive" vs. "Being Expensive"
**Source:** Bill Miller
High-ROIC businesses at optically high multiples can be bargains if returns are sustainable. Conversely, "cheap" stocks with deteriorating returns are expensive. Ask: Is this looking expensive or being expensive?

### 19. Destination Analysis / The 10-Year Question
**Source:** Nomad, Omar Malik, Peter Thiel
What does this business look like in 10 years? If the destination is knowable, the current price represents a mispricing of the journey. Ask: Can I describe the destination with confidence? What's the implied return?

### 20. Base Rate Analysis / Outside View
**Source:** Mauboussin
Anchor on what typically happens to companies in this position before building a bottom-up model. Ask: What do base rates say? Am I implicitly assuming this company defies them?

### 21. Fifty Cent Dollars — Price-to-Value Discipline
**Source:** Nomad (Sleep)
Purchase assets at significant discount to intrinsic value. This is the valuation check after any framework generates an idea. Ask: Even if the thesis is right, is there adequate margin of safety?

## IV. MANAGEMENT & GOVERNANCE

### 22. Owner-Operator Alignment / Founder Economics
**Source:** Nomad (Sleep)
Intrinsically motivated founders make decisions incomprehensible to standard models but enormously value-creative. Ask: Would the CEO still be doing this with 10x their current wealth?

### 23. Find the .400 Hitters — Management as Capital Cycle Navigators
**Source:** Omar Malik, Buffett
Best managers act counter-cyclically: pulling back when others add supply, acquiring when others are distressed. Ask: Has this team demonstrated counter-cyclical capital allocation?

### 24. CEO Behavior & Management Signaling (Inverse)
**Source:** Marathon
Read management communication inversely. Red flags: buybacks at high prices, empire-building M&A, excessive comp, CFO departure. Ask: What would management say if fully honest? How does that compare to what they're saying?

### 25. Governance Transformation as Catalyst
**Source:** Marathon
Companies at deep discounts to book due to poor governance, with visible catalysts for improvement. Ask: Is the discount governance-driven? Is there a visible catalyst?

## V. BEHAVIORAL & STRUCTURAL EDGE

### 26. Behavioral Advantage / Exploiting the Agency Problem
**Source:** Nomad, Marathon, Omar Malik
The investment industry's structure creates systematic mispricings: short horizons, benchmark-hugging, career risk. Ask: Is this opportunity time-arbitrage that most capital can't access?

### 27. Deep Simplicity / The Deception of Complexity
**Source:** Nomad (Sleep)
Best investments are deeply simple businesses the market overcomplicates. Ask: Can I explain this in two sentences? Is the market overcomplicating something simple?

### 28. Conviction vs. Belief Superiority / Epistemic Humility
**Source:** Marathon
Confidence is testable and open to disconfirmation. Belief superiority is closed and fragile. Ask: Am I seeking disconfirming evidence? What's the strongest argument against?

### 29. Process vs. Outcome Distinction
**Source:** Bill Miller
A decision is bad if the process was bad, regardless of outcome. Ask: Was the process sound? Am I falling into outcome bias?

### 30. Inactivity as Alpha / The Compounding Holding Period
**Source:** Nomad (Sleep), Buffett/Munger
Most trading destroys value. Ask: Is this a hold-forever compounder? Am I tempted to trade because of noise?

## VI. PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION

### 31. Dynamic Allocation — Concentrated Winners + Tail Positions + Baskets
**Source:** Omar Malik, Buffett
Three tools: concentrated compounder positions (10-20%+, hold for decades), tail positions (1-3%, let winners emerge), baskets (express industry-level theses when you can't pick the winner). Ask: What role does this position play?

## VII. TECHNOLOGY CYCLE & SCARCITY MIGRATION

### 32. Scarcity Migration — Where Value Concentrates in Technology Cycles
**Source:** Ian McDonald
Each cycle: scarce input → abstraction → abundance → scarcity migrates one layer down. Durable returns accrue to those who identify where scarcity migrates after technology begins to work. Ask: Where does this company sit in the sequence? Is the market still pricing the old bottleneck?

### 33. Cascading Physical Constraints — Bits Collide With Atoms
**Source:** Ian St. Bernard
Digital scales exponentially; physical scales linearly. The mismatch is where scarcity enters. Constraints form a cascading system: compute → memory → energy → thermal/water. Each solution creates the next constraint. Ask: Is this company in a current or next-stage physical constraint layer? Can the bottleneck be solved with capital alone?

### 34. Layer-Straddling — Companies That Bridge Scarcity and Abundance
**Source:** Ian St. Bernard
Hyperscalers straddle both layers simultaneously — extracting rents from constraint today while retaining the option to monetize abundance tomorrow. Ask: Does this company have positions in both layers? Is the market penalizing capex that's actually investment in future abundance?

### 35. The Abundance Question — Who Wins When Tokens Are Cheap?
**Source:** Ian St. Bernard
When compute is cheap and intelligence is free, the bottleneck moves to distribution, data, trust, and physical moats. Current posture: invest in scarcity, avoid abundance, monitor for phase transition. Ask: Is this a scarcity-layer play (investable now) or abundance-layer (premature)?

### 36. The Physical Tether Filter — Does This Business Touch Atoms?
**Source:** Ian St. Bernard
Companies with physical tethers (land, water, grid, materials, permits) are harder to disrupt. Companies without them face AI substitution risk. Ask: Does this business have a physical tether? If not, what prevents AI from replicating the value proposition at near-zero cost?

### 37. Aggregation Theory — The Counter-Thesis for Abundance Winners
**Source:** Ben Thompson
When supply becomes abundant, value accrues to demand aggregators. This is the primary counter-thesis to "avoid all abundance plays." When frameworks collide (scarcity says avoid, aggregation says buy), resolve the tension for the specific company. Ask: Is this an aggregator or a supplier? When the key input becomes cheap, does this company become more or less valuable?

## QUICK REFERENCE: PATTERN-MATCHING CHECKLIST

**Capital Cycle:** Where in the cycle? Supply entering or leaving? Consolidation underway? Zombies alive?

**Moat:** ROIC vs WACC spread? Fade rate? Five Forces? MES/TAM? Network effects (type/strength)? Switching costs? Real pricing power? Disruption threat? Scale economies shared?

**Valuation:** Below replacement cost? Description accurate? Looking vs being expensive? 10-year destination? Base rates? Margin of safety?

**Management:** Owner-operator? Counter-cyclical allocator? Red flags? Governance catalyst?

**Behavioral:** Time-arbitrage? Deep simplicity? Seeking disconfirmation? Hold-forever compounder?

**Scarcity/Abundance:** Position in scarcity → abundance sequence? Physical constraint layer? Local/non-fungible? Physical tether? Layer-straddler? Cascading next constraint?

**Abundance Risk:** Scarcity play (now) or abundance play (premature)? Physical tether? AI replication risk? Aggregator or supplier? Frameworks colliding?

**Portfolio Role:** Concentrated compounder, tail position, or basket?
