## Summary

**Context.** Verisk is down ~45% over the past year, and almost none of that is the business — revenue and margins moved the right way and estimates are higher, not lower. The de-rating is factor-driven: the market repriced the entire software complex for AI-disruption risk and sold the durable moats alongside the fragile ones. VRSK screens as "software" with a historically premium multiple, so it was a natural target for indiscriminate selling. The multiple has gone from a 5-year average near 43x to roughly 23-25x forward — a high-ROIC, recurring-revenue compounder trading near the low end of its decade.

**Thesis.** Verisk owns the loss-cost and actuarial datasets the U.S. P&C insurance industry prices, reserves, and files rate changes against. That data is the reference the industry and its regulators verify against, and it compounds: every policy written and claim paid feeds it, and no competitor can reconstruct decades of accumulated loss experience. This is the cleanest example on our sheet of a moat AI strengthens rather than erodes — an agent can draft a rate filing but cannot manufacture the ground-truth loss data the filing must cite, nor assume liability for being wrong. The market is pricing VRSK as a slow-growth software name caught in the AI downdraft; I think it is a verification-data utility whose moat is widening while the multiple has halved.

**Price problem.** Not statistically cheap — ~23x forward earnings, ~8x sales, on mid-single-digit revenue growth. The fair bear case: a 5-7% grower may not deserve 40x, so the de-rating could be a correct repricing rather than an overshoot. The return does not require the old multiple back: high-single-digit EPS growth + ~1% yield + buyback + partial re-rating off a 45%-drawdown low compounds to a mid-teens IRR, with a fortress moat as downside protection.

**Rating: Buy.** +25bps active, cheap-and-durable core of the software re-entry. Compounder-and-hold, not a trade.

## Our Thesis

Verisk's actuarial and loss-cost data is a verification-grade moat that AI strengthens rather than threatens, and the franchise compounds adjusted EPS at a high-single-digit rate through the cycle. At ~23x forward vs a ~43x five-year average, the market prices it as an AI-disruption casualty; the moat is widening, and EPS growth plus a partial re-rating delivers a mid-teens three-year return. Falsifiable: in twelve months, if revenue growth held mid-single-digits, margins expanded, and the multiple did not de-rate further, the thesis is intact.

## Thesis Tracker

1. **Multiple re-rates toward history.** ~23-25x vs ~43x 5-yr average. Not underwriting a full round-trip; watching whether the gap narrows as AI-fear fades. Monitor vs peer multiples and own history.
2. **EPS keeps compounding high-single-digits regardless of multiple.** 7-13% EPS on 5-7% revenue via margin expansion (44%→47% EBIT) and buyback. The engine that makes us right even on partial re-rating. Watch margin and share count.
3. **Recurring mix holds and AI is additive, not substitutive.** Watch for generative tools letting insurers self-serve VRSK analytics (downside) vs AI features converting to billable revenue (upside).

## Proof Points

### The data is what the industry is measured against
Verisk's core datasets (loss costs, ISO forms, cat models) are the reference layer U.S. P&C insurers price against and file rate changes on with state regulators. The moat is the standard-setter / verification position — the authority everyone checks against — a coordination position no entrant can bootstrap and no model can synthesize.

### The data compounds, and the compounding is exclusive
Every policy priced and claim paid deepens the loss-experience dataset. A competitor cannot reconstruct decades of cleaned, regulator-accepted loss data; neither can an insurer, which is why they buy rather than build. The "data-that-proves" pattern: compounds, and gets more valuable as more decisions depend on it — the inverse of commodity/scrapeable data that AI devalues.

### AI is additive, not substitutive
An LLM can draft a filing or summarize a cat model but must cite ground-truth loss data to do so, and Verisk owns that input. AI raises demand for the verified data layer beneath it. Upside (to confirm, not assume): Verisk monetizing its own AI layer (Ignite, partner integrations) as incremental billable functionality.

### The de-rating is factor-driven, not fundamental
Over the year the stock fell ~45%, revenue rose and margins expanded; forward estimates are higher. A drawdown that size with fundamentals moving the opposite way is externally-imposed multiple compression (software-complex AI-fear), not deterioration inside the business.

## The Business

**How it makes money.** Subscription/transactional access to insurance data, analytics, and workflow software, overwhelmingly U.S. P&C insurers. Highly recurring, capital-light — data collected once through the industry's operations, sold many times. ~$3.2B 2026E revenue at ~46% EBIT margin, margins still expanding; 5-7% revenue converts to high-single-digit-to-low-teens EPS via operating leverage and buyback.

**Moat.** Data plus standard-setter position; ROIC far above WACC with slow fade. Structural risk to watch is regulatory — antitrust/pricing scrutiny or a carrier consortium pooling its own loss data — both high-friction, neither materialized at scale.

**Growth vectors.** Price (mission-criticality supports increases); analytics/workflow layer on top of data (cat modeling, claims, anti-fraud — raises revenue per customer); AI layer (billable generative features in Ignite / partner integrations). Historical revenue growth slow (~1.4% 2021-26 CAGR, depressed partly by divestitures); the bull case needs no acceleration, but any re-acceleration is unpriced.

**Management & capital allocation.** Reshaped into a focused insurance-data pure-play via divestitures; steady buyback + modest dividend; low capital intensity, high FCF conversion. Watch M&A discipline and avoidance of empire-building outside the core. Recent record (narrowing the business) is the behavior we want.

**Valuation.** $176.84 = ~23x 2026E / ~20x 2027E adj EPS, ~8x forward sales, ~16-18x EV/EBIT, vs ~43x 5-yr avg P/E. SBC modest vs the high-multiple software cohort, so GAAP-to-adjusted gap is narrow. Conservative Buy math: hold multiple flat at ~23x, let ~9% EPS + ~1% yield + buyback run → low-double-digit IRR; allow partial re-rating as AI-fear discount fades → mid-teens. Not modeling a return to 43x; that's upside, not the case.

## What Could Go Wrong

1. **The multiple was the anomaly and ~23x is the new normal.** A 5-7% grower may not warrant a premium multiple; if the de-rating is correct repricing, the re-rating leg never comes and we're left with a ~10% IRR. Central risk; thesis built not to require reversion.
2. **Growth stays stuck low-single-digits.** If price increases meet resistance and the analytics/AI layer adds no growth vector, EPS growth slows toward the revenue line. Watch organic revenue and net price realization.
3. **AI-monetization proof never shows in the numbers.** Ignite/partner AI features are flagged as upside, not foundation. If AI never converts to billable revenue — or lets large carriers self-serve analytics they used to buy — the "additive" pillar weakens. Not yet confirmed in primary results; a known gap in this initiation.
4. **Being wrong by omission.** As a Buy, the symmetric risk: if software-fear lifts fast, a 45%-discounted fortress re-rates quickly and 25bps is too small. Be ready to add on confirmation.

## Conclusion

Buy, +25bps, core ballast in the software re-entry. Verisk owns verification-grade data the insurance industry is measured against; the moat widens as AI raises demand for trusted ground-truth; the market handed us a 45% discount for reasons external to the franchise. Upgrade conviction/size if the multiple re-rates with margins and growth intact, or if AI monetization shows up as billable revenue; revisit if organic growth stalls below ~4% or the standard-setter position faces regulatory or consortium challenge. Next decision point: the next two prints — margin, share count, any concrete AI revenue disclosure.
