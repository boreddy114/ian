> Decision note: rating moved from a proposed +20bps Buy to **Neutral** after a four-prompt expert pressure test plus primary-source (Quartr) work on segment revenue, the Q4 FY26 call, and Salesforce's own life-sciences disclosures. The moat confirmed; the price/growth case did not clear.

## Summary

**Context.** Veeva is down about 26% over the past year and ~48% below its 2024 high (~$160 vs a $310 high), caught in the software-complex de-rating on AI-disruption fear and a specific "late to AI" worry while Salesforce presses its CRM franchise. The reported business is intact: FY26 (year ended Jan 31) revenue grew 16% to $3.20B at a ~45% non-GAAP operating margin, with $1.39B of free cash flow and ~$6.6B of net cash. The question is what a durable-core franchise is worth when the contestable edge is live and growth is guided to step down.

**Thesis.** Expert and primary-source work confirms the heart of the bull case: the validated regulated-records core — clinical, regulatory submissions, pharmacovigilance, and quality on Vault — is genuinely durable through AI, protected by re-validation cost, audit-trail continuity, and multi-party coordination; no top-20 is evaluating moving a core system off Veeva; and AI runs on top of the system of record rather than against it. The investable question is price and growth — and on both I land Neutral. Growth is guided down from ~16% to ~13% (a multiple problem under our own principle), and the deceleration trips a diagnostic flag in our durability framework that the work has only partly cleared. The contestable edge (CRM, now possibly commercial content) is live, and the re-rating catalyst is the furthest out of the names we are weighing.

**Price.** At ~18x forward non-GAAP and ~13–15x EV/FCF, with ~$6.6B net cash and ~44% margins, Veeva is genuinely de-rated. But revenue growth drives the multiple and the step-down is guided, so there is no re-rating to underwrite. The most one would underwrite is the earnings algorithm — low-teens EPS plus buyback — and even that is gated on the core continuing to compound. Cheap does not function as a catalyst.

**Rating: Neutral.** No position. The honest landing after the pressure test: a confirmed-durable core we would like to own at the right evidence, held at Neutral pending resolution of the growth-deceleration flag and the contested-edge trajectory. It is the toughest of the out-of-favor software names to underwrite today — the moat is real, but the live, unresolved negatives are too many to pay for ahead of the print.

## Our View

Veeva's regulated systems of record are protected by re-validation cost, audit-trail continuity, and multi-party coordination that AI does not erode — confirmed, not assumed: expert work finds displacement of a validated core system "extremely rare," with no top-20 evaluating a move, and management and customers alike describe AI as running on top of the record rather than replacing it. The mix is shifting by design toward that durable core as the contestable CRM piece shrinks. That makes the moat real. It does not, on its own, make the stock a buy. Growth is guided down from ~16% to ~13%; revenue growth drives the multiple, so the de-rate to ~18x is in large part an appropriate repricing rather than pure AI panic. And a structural deceleration is itself a flag in our durability framework — it can mark benign maturation or early moat-monetization erosion. The work resolves it mostly benign (the core is stable at ~21%, the slowdown is guided and Commercial-mix-driven) but leaves one real forward risk unresolved: AI-lateness is materializing as a cap on expansion and cross-sell at the edge. We are Neutral. Upgrade to Buy if the June 3 print shows the core holding the high-teens with the guided step-down proving conservative, the edge staying bounded, and AI beginning to monetize. Move to avoid if the core rolls toward mid-teens, the edge widens into content or clinical, or subscription gross retention cracks.

## Thesis Tracker — What Moves Us Off Neutral

1. **The deceleration flag: does the guided step-down prove real, or conservative?** Reported revenue has held a stable ~16% for eight quarters and R&D & Quality subscription is stable at ~21%; the step-down to ~13% lives in the FY27 guide. The June 3 Q1 FY27 print is the first test — watch total revenue against the $855–858M guide and, more important, R&D & Quality subscription growth. A core holding the high-teens with a guide beat resolves the flag benign and is an upgrade trigger; a core rolling toward mid-teens resolves it bearish.
2. **The contested edge: bounded, or widening?** CRM (~20% of revenue) is conceded and shrinking toward ~10% by 2030 — watch the ~14-of-top-20 commitment count hold. The new item is the beachhead: Salesforce has guided a regulated (promotional) content-management product for H2, aimed at Veeva's PromoMats turf. Watch that launch and whether any top-20 adopts it. The edge staying at CRM is supportive; extending into content is a negative.
3. **AI: from concession to monetization, and does it stop capping cross-sell?** AI is given away today (free suite plus tokens ~2 years) to retain CRM; monetization is tokenized, FY28+, excluded from the $6B target. Watch for measurable AI revenue and, critically, for the expert-flagged expansion friction — longer cycles, deferred discretionary modules — to ease rather than deepen in the protected core.

## Proof Points

*Trailing-180-day expert-call questions cluster into a handful of concerns, pressure-tested against expert transcripts, the FY26 filings and Q4 call, the reported segment data, and Salesforce's own life-sciences disclosures. Ordered by how directly they bear on the rating.*

### Concern 1 — Salesforce is taking CRM, and the contest is starting to widen. My judgment: real and bounded at the core, but the edge may be extending into commercial content.

Of the top-20 pharma, Veeva expects ~14 to commit to Vault CRM and ~6 to move to Salesforce; CRM is ~20% of revenue, shrinking toward ~10% by 2030 by design, and the loss is a low-single-digit revenue headwind through 2030. That part is bounded and confirmed — three of four top-20 in recent checks chose Veeva on cost, regulatory fit, and an immature Salesforce product, and the committed migrations are underway on a live platform. The new development is the beachhead. Salesforce's regulated-industries head told investors in January that health and life sciences is now a $4.7B ARR business (inside a company guiding to $41.5B this year and $60B by 2030), that everything generally available today is commercial and data-layer, and — the line to watch — that "regulated content management" is "coming quickly" in the second half, aimed squarely at Veeva's PromoMats turf. The contest is extending within the contestable edge, from CRM into commercial content. Where the bound holds: there is still no Salesforce clinical, RIM, Safety, or quality system-of-record product, none on the stated roadmap, and Salesforce positions itself as an agentic and data layer rather than a records replacement — the validated core sits outside its footprint. The edge is contested and possibly widening; the moat is not breached.

### Concern 2 — Veeva is late to AI, and pharma is routing budget around it. My judgment: AI runs on top of the core, but the lateness is materializing as a real cap on expansion.

Pharma is building AI on frontier models (Anthropic's Claude, AWS Bedrock) and adopting AI-natives such as Copli and OpenEvidence (the latter ~$12B private), and the consensus is that Veeva was late. The durable answer holds: customers describe Veeva as the system of record they build AI on top of, and the CEO frames LLMs as "an engine… the new AWS," infrastructure Veeva builds on, with AI "not going to replace core systems of record like SAP, Workday, or Veeva." The lateness does not threaten the validated core. Where it bites — and this is part of why we are Neutral, not long — is the expansion engine: experts describe longer sales cycles on net-new and non-core add-on modules, deferred discretionary modules, and the end of "automatic" wall-to-wall standardization as customers reallocate budget to internal AI. One former large-pharma director called Veeva "significantly behind" its customers' internal efforts. The base is resilient; the cross-sell the growth case needs is being capped at the margin.

### Concern 3 — growth has slowed. My judgment: the crux of the Neutral — both a multiple problem and a framework flag, and only partly cleared.

Two things are true at once. First, the multiple problem: revenue growth drives the multiple, the FY27 guide steps growth down from ~16% to ~13%, and the CFO removed the cushion — the FY26 outperformance was Crossix-driven and is not expected to repeat. So the de-rate to ~18x is substantially an appropriate repricing, and there is no re-rating to underwrite until the market sees where stable growth settles. Second, the framework flag: in our durability work a structural deceleration is a diagnostic warning that the moat's monetization may be weakening, to be distinguished from benign maturation. The reported data argues mostly benign — total revenue has held a stable ~16% for eight quarters and R&D & Quality subscription is steady at ~21%, so the slowdown is guided and Commercial-mix-driven rather than core erosion. But the flag is not fully cleared: the FY27 step-down is guided and untested, and the AI-capped expansion in Concern 2 is exactly the mechanism by which benign maturation could turn real. Amber, not green. Where I concede: if core subscription growth rolls toward mid-teens on June 3, the flag turns red, ~18x is not cheap, and this is a value trap rather than a value name.

### Concern 4 — pricing pushback. My judgment: concentrated in the contestable CRM renewal; the validated core retains its pricing.

Expert calls flag emerging pushback and visible concessions — subsidized migration, free AI and tokens for ~two years, the CEO personally negotiating — but these are concentrated in the CRM renewal, where Salesforce now gives customers an alternative and therefore leverage. The validated core is a different market: switching means re-validation, the company leads its stated competitive factors with regulatory-compliance verification ahead of price, and subscription gross margin of 75.5% (expanding) shows no core-pricing erosion. The pressure test corroborates a resilient, sticky base — renewals are not narrowing, cancellations are rare. Where I concede: a spread of pushback from CRM into the core would be an early moat signal; gross retention is the number to watch, and the kill condition is sub-90% for two consecutive quarters.

### Concern 5 — cross-sell and the data business. My judgment: core standardization is deepening; the data layer is the contestable soft spot, with an unresolved tension.

On the core, standardization is deepening — products-per-customer in R&D is rising (about 2.6 to 3.4), clinical is consolidating onto Veeva from IQVIA and Medidata, and the company continues to win top-20 mandates in Safety, RTSM, and quality. The soft spot is Data Cloud, and here the sources conflict: management called Crossix a "breakout year" with a long runway, while expert calls say rivals such as Ipsos and Kantar have caught or passed Crossix and that Nitro offers limited value to a top-20. Reference data is also the category most exposed to frontier-model approximation. This tension is flagged for more work. Where I concede: the data business is genuinely contestable against a larger IQVIA, and a stall in core cross-sell — not only at the data edge — would remove the growth support the Neutral assumes is intact.

## The Business

**How it makes money.** Veeva sells industry-cloud software, data, and consulting to life sciences — about 1,552 customers, with the top-20 pharma roughly half of revenue. Two segments: R&D & Quality Solutions (~53% of revenue, the faster grower) spans clinical (eTMF, EDC, CTMS), regulatory submissions (RIM), pharmacovigilance (Safety), and Quality (QMS), all on Vault. Commercial Solutions (~45%) is Vault CRM and legacy Salesforce-based CRM, plus Medical, PromoMats content, and the Crossix/OpenData/Link/Compass data assets. Subscription is ~84% of revenue. With the exception of legacy CRM, everything runs on Vault.

**Revenue trajectory — the deceleration is guided, not yet reported.** The reason the flag is amber rather than red is in the printed numbers: total revenue growth has held a stable mid-teens for two years, and the step-down sits in the FY27 guide. Q1 FY25 laps a depressed FY24 quarter (a 2023 pharma-spending air pocket), so the underlying trend is the steady ~14–17% that follows.

Underneath the blend, the split is the whole point. In FY26 the R&D & Quality core subscription grew ~21% (and ~20.8% in Q4), stable across every quarterly cut, while Commercial subscription grew ~14% and decelerated to ~11.5% in Q4 as CRM matured and Crossix lapped hard comps. So the protected two-thirds is compounding ~21% and is not what is slowing; the consolidated step-down to the ~13% guide is Commercial-mix plus conservative guidance. The caution the framework imposes: the FY27 guide is management's own signal of a step-down, the CFO does not expect the FY26 beat to repeat, and a guided number is not a reported one — so we treat the core's durability as confirmed through FY26 and the FY27 trajectory as the open question.

**The earnings algorithm.** If owned, the return would rest on converting low-teens recurring-revenue growth into low-teens non-GAAP EPS growth, with the durable core compounding while the contestable CRM piece is shrunk by design — not on a re-rating.

| Driver | Contribution | Nature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Recurring (subscription) revenue | ~+13% (guide) | Core sub ~21% and stable; Commercial ~12–14% and softening; blend guided to ~13% |
| Margin | ~+1 to +2% | Structural — Salesforce-royalty elimination, scale; ~44% non-GAAP op margin |
| Buyback | ~+1% | Recurring — $2B authorization, funded from ~$6.6B net cash |
| AI monetization | option | Upside — tokenized, FY28+, excluded from the $6B 2030 target |
| Non-GAAP EPS | ~+9% guide / low-teens | FY27E ~$8.85; guide is "closest to the pin," not set up for a repeat beat |

Margin expansion is partly mechanical as customers migrate off the Salesforce-based CRM and the royalties Veeva pays Salesforce fall away. FCF is ~43% of revenue against ~$6.6B of net cash, no debt, funding the $2B buyback with room to spare. The honest constraint: the algorithm only delivers low-teens if the core holds, and the guide itself steps the headline down.

**Competitive position, the moat, and the Salesforce beachhead.** Veeva's 10-K leads its competitive factors with regulatory-compliance verification and domain expertise. In CRM it competes with Salesforce; in data with IQVIA (larger incumbent), Ipsos, and Definitive Healthcare; in development and quality no single vendor spans the suite (IQVIA, Dassault's Medidata, Oracle, OpenText compete app by app). The durable advantage is the validated Vault system of record — switching means re-validating against 21 CFR Part 11 and EU Annex 11, preserving audit-trail continuity, and re-coordinating sponsors, CROs, sites, and regulators. On the beachhead: Salesforce is material to Veeva but Veeva is immaterial to Salesforce — its entire HLS ARR (~$4.7B, much of it health-plan/provider business unrelated to Veeva) is a small slice of a ~$41.5B company, so the CRM share shift cannot be expressed through Salesforce stock. Salesforce's commercial-content roadmap for H2 is the genuine watch-item, since it targets the contestable PromoMats edge; the validated core remains outside its product line and stated ambition.

**Management and capital allocation.** Founder-CEO Peter Gassner (ex-Salesforce SVP of Technology) owns ~9% and is paid in premium-priced options vesting through 2030, compensation tied to five-year stock compounding. Veeva is a Delaware PBC; it converted to single-class voting in 2023, declassified the board, and runs a $2B buyback against ~$6.6B of net cash. The one genuine capital-allocation risk is the $100B-plus horizontal "New Markets" ambition, where Veeva carries no regulatory moat and faces Salesforce and Microsoft directly.

**Valuation.** At ~$160, ~18x forward FY27 non-GAAP, ~20x trailing, ~13–15x EV/FCF, on ~$6.6B net cash; the GAAP multiple is richer (~29x trailing on $5.44) because SBC drives the gap. A genuine de-rate — ~27% YTD and ~48% off the high. Not, by itself, a reason to own it. Revenue growth drives the multiple, the guide steps growth down, and our principle is not to underwrite multiple expansion until the market is comfortable with where stable growth settles or sees a path to reacceleration. At ~18x for a confirmed-durable core compounding ~21% behind a real moat, the value is genuine and the downside is cushioned by cash and cash flow — but the catalyst is a print, not a price, and the work leaves enough unresolved that we wait for it.

## What Could Go Wrong — and What We Have to Resolve

1. **The guided step-down proves real and the core rolls toward mid-teens.** If R&D & Quality subscription decelerates from ~21% as the law of large numbers and AI-capped cross-sell bite, the framework flag turns red, ~18x is not cheap, and this is a value trap. The central downside, first tested June 3.
2. **The contested edge widens.** Salesforce's H2 regulated-content (PromoMats) launch converts, or Agentforce eventually presses into clinical; either stops the contestable share from shrinking and undercuts the mix-shift.
3. **The data business is commoditized.** Crossix and Nitro are contestable today, and management's "breakout" framing conflicts with expert reports of share loss to Ipsos and Kantar — unresolved. If frontier models plus open data match compliance-grade reference data, the Data moat erodes.
4. **We are wrong to wait.** The symmetric risk to a Neutral: if the step-down is conservative, the core holds ~21%, and AI monetizes, a cheap durable compounder re-rates and we miss it. This is why the position is Neutral-with-an-upgrade-path rather than avoid.

## Conclusion — Neutral, Flag for More Work

Neutral, no position. The pressure test confirmed the thing that matters most — the validated regulated-records core is durable, AI runs on top of it, and no top-20 is moving a core system — but it also surfaced enough live, unresolved negatives to hold rather than buy: growth is guided to step down (a multiple problem under our own principle), the deceleration trips a durability-framework flag that is amber rather than cleared, the contestable edge is live and may be widening into commercial content, and the re-rating catalyst is the furthest out of the names we are weighing. The work to do before this becomes a position: (1) resolve the deceleration flag on the June 3 Q1 FY27 print — does R&D & Quality subscription hold the high-teens and does the guide prove conservative; (2) resolve the Crossix tension between management's "breakout" and the experts' share-loss reports; (3) track Salesforce's H2 regulated-content launch and any top-20 adoption; (4) watch subscription gross retention for any spread of pricing pushback from CRM into the core. Upgrade to Buy on a clean core print with a bounded edge; move to avoid if the core decelerates, the edge widens, or retention cracks. The framework working as intended — a cheap, genuinely durable name held at Neutral because the evidence is not yet sufficient to underwrite the growth.
