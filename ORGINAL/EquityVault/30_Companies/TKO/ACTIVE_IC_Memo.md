## Executive Summary

1. **TKO owns the two dominant year-round live sports franchises.** UFC (32% of revenue, ~58% EBITDA margins) and WWE (43%, 50%+) run a combined 200+ events per year with no off-season. There is no close substitute for either property. The event calendars are full — growth comes from each event being worth more, not from adding events.

2. **Five visible growth levers compound the core business at 9-10% annually.** Media rights escalators are contractual (5%/year built into the Paramount+ deal). Ticket pricing has significant headroom vs. NFL, NBA, and F1. International site fees are scaling as sovereign-funded tourism entities bid for a limited number of event slots. Sponsorship is expanding into new categories at 25%+ annual growth. And the CBS simulcast — if it works — transforms the 2033 renewal.

3. **The UP-C corporate structure is a presentation issue, not an economic one.** We traced the full distribution waterfall and confirmed zero variance between Class A free cash flow and a hypothetical normal C-corp. The reported adjusted FCF headline is overstated because it imputes a 12% effective tax rate, but applying a normalized 25-29% rate yields the same per-share economics. The real governance concerns are about control and dilution, not tax mechanics.

4. **At 19x EV/EBITDA, we are paying a fair price for premium assets with contractual growth.** The 40x normalized P/E sounds expensive in isolation, but these are trophy assets with visible multi-year compounding and a 2033 renewal catalyst that could step-function the earnings power.

## I. UFC — 32% of Revenue

UFC runs 43 events per year across arenas worldwide. The schedule is roughly one event per week, year-round. There is no off-season. The business model is asset-light: TKO rents venues, stages the event, and leaves. Capital expenditure was $70 million on $4.7 billion of revenue in 2025.

### Media Rights

The $7.7 billion Paramount+ deal ($1.1B/year) has built-in annual escalators estimated at 5%. This is contractual revenue growth that requires nothing to go right. UFC media rights grow from $876 million in 2025 to approximately $1.5 billion by 2033 on escalators alone.

The 2033 renewal is the single most important variable in the thesis. Sports media rights have roughly doubled at every major renewal cycle: NFL at 2.3x, NBA at 2.5x, UFC's own domestic deal at 3.7x. We reviewed expert transcripts from former Fox Sports and Paramount executives. The consistent estimate is 1.5-2.5x, driven by the structural scarcity of year-round live content and the subscriber retention value UFC provides to Paramount+. Experts note that Paramount measures success through churn reduction and subscriber lifetime value — the year-round schedule means there is always a reason not to cancel. The fight cadence creates up to 20 minutes of premium ad inventory per hour, making UFC unusually valuable for advertisers.

The CBS simulcast tests whether UFC can be a mainstream broadcast property, which determines the renewal's magnitude. UFC 326 on March 7 averaged 2.47 million viewers — the best UFC linear audience in a decade, with adults 18-34 up 208%. This was a solid debut. The next two data points matter more: UFC 327 Miami (April 11, two title fights) and the White House event (June 14, CBS simulcast confirmed). A trajectory of 2.5M → 3.5M → 6M+ validates a 1.5-2x renewal. A plateau at 2-3M suggests 1.2x, which reduces our 2036 media estimate by roughly $500 million.

### Ticket Pricing

UFC events are priced well below comparable live sports. Our per-event pricing models show weighted average face values of $424 for a Fight Night and $541 for a numbered event. NFL playoffs run $500-800, NBA courtside $1,000+, F1 Paddock Club $5,000-15,000. On a per-hour-of-live-action basis, championship UFC is roughly half the cost of Wimbledon or F1. A 5-10% annual increase in face value compounds to 60-160% over a decade, adding $200-400 million to annual gate revenue on the same number of events.

We backtested our event tracker against Q4 2025: the model estimated $70.3 million versus $72.0 million reported in live events revenue, a 2.4% variance. The tracker uses face-value ticket data, venue capacities, and state athletic commission attendance figures — all independent of TKO's reported revenue. Sell-side firms have historical event-level data but use it as reference, not for forward estimation. The per-event data also reveals something the top-down models miss: UFC economics are bimodal. A numbered event in Miami generates $10.3 million in gate revenue. An Apex studio card the week before generates zero. Both count as "one event" in a top-down model. Knowing the mix before the quarter is reported is the edge.

Expert transcripts caution that aggressive premium pricing risks alienating the casual fan base in a downturn. The offsetting factor is that removing the $80 PPV paywall via Paramount+ is expanding the top of the funnel, supporting higher arena prices at the premium end while streaming serves price-sensitive fans at home.

### International Site Fees (FIPs)

Cities and countries pay TKO upfront to bring events to their market. Management guided to $300M+ in 2026 (from ~$240M in 2025) with a $380-420M target by 2030. Saudi Arabia is scaling from 1 to 3 events per year at $15-25M each. Abu Dhabi runs $8-15M. Macau and Shanghai are new markets at $3-8M. European and Australian events run $2-5M.

Expert calls confirm the structure: FIPs are fixed upfront guarantees, and host nations often subsidize travel and operational costs on top of the headline payment. UFC has a significant operational advantage over competing sports properties — rent an arena for a night, perform, leave. F1 requires track buildouts, road closures, and tens of millions in infrastructure. This makes UFC a more cost-effective product for host cities and keeps the pipeline of bidders growing faster than TKO can supply event slots.

### Sponsorship

TKO achieved over $450 million in partnerships revenue in 2025 and raised its 2030 target to $1.2 billion. UFC's sponsorship-per-TV-viewer ratio ($0.27) is roughly half of F1's ($0.52), despite a younger and faster-growing audience. Management is opening new categories: fintech (Polymarket), delivery (DoorDash), automotive (Ram), enterprise tech (IBM, Meta). Closing even half the monetization gap to F1 implies $500M+ in UFC sponsorship alone, before counting WWE.

### Audience

UFC skews 75-80% male with a 25-44 core. Roughly 50% of Americans aged 18-24 identify as MMA fans. The women's divisions have competitive depth but lack a crossover star since Ronda Rousey, whose UFC 193 drew 56,000 fans in Melbourne. Moving the female audience from 20% to 35% would expand the addressable audience by 15-20% and unlock female-targeted sponsor categories. This is upside optionality, not in the base case.

Combat sports have had cycles before — Bruce Lee in the 1970s, the Karate Kid era in the 1980s, early UFC's "human cockfighting" reputation in the 1990s, the McGregor-driven PPV boom in the 2010s. Each prior cycle was driven by individual icons and faded when the icon departed. The current era is structurally different: UFC is an institutional platform with 43 annual events, $7.7B in locked domestic media rights, and state athletic commission sanctioning in all 50 states. The sport has moved from star-dependent to infrastructure-dependent.

## II. WWE — 43% of Revenue

WWE is the largest segment by revenue and the most misunderstood. The product — scripted outcomes, theatrical characters, melodramatic storylines — invites dismissal. That dismissal is wrong.

WWE is not a sport. It is serialized entertainment performed live, with athletic performance as the medium. The better analogy is Marvel: proprietary characters (Undertaker, The Rock, Cena, Roman Reigns) with multi-generational story arcs, merchandising, and global licensing. The characters are intellectual property, not interchangeable athletes. There is no PFL equivalent for professional wrestling. Nobody can create a competing WWE.

The Netflix deal is the anchor. Raw moved to Netflix in January 2025 and delivered 525 million hours streamed in its first year, appearing in the weekly Top 10 in 30+ countries. The deal locks in a significant media rights step-up with annual escalators. SmackDown remains on USA Network. Premium Live Events (WrestleMania, SummerSlam, Royal Rumble) air on Peacock domestically and Netflix internationally.

WWE tours approximately 170 events per year — far more than UFC's 43. The touring model generates steady gate revenue across arenas globally. Saudi Arabia pays $50M+ per event for Premium Live Events, scaling from 1 event in 2025 to 3 in 2026. Sponsorship is growing at 30%+ annually off a lower base as Netflix drives mainstream visibility into demographics that didn't previously watch wrestling.

The female audience is already approximately 40% — double UFC's share. This gives WWE a broader demographic base and access to advertising categories that UFC cannot yet reach. John Cena's 2025 retirement tour was the highest-grossing stretch of arena dates in WWE history. The post-Cena question is whether the institutional platform — weekly serialized content, the NXT development pipeline, Netflix global distribution — sustains engagement without a generational star. The platform has never been stronger, but the test is ahead.

WWE EBITDA margins have expanded from roughly 40% to 50%+ in two years under Endeavor management, with estimates approaching the high 50s within 2-3 years.

## III. IMG and On Location — 23% of Revenue

Endeavor contributed IMG, On Location, and PBR to TKO in February 2025 for approximately $3.25 billion in OpCo units. This was a common control transaction with no independent valuation.

**On Location** is the premium hospitality business managing VIP packages for the Super Bowl, FIFA World Cup, Olympics, college football playoffs, and now UFC and WWE events. It is genuinely synergistic — it powers the $920-$2,730 VIP packages in our per-event models, and TKO captures the full revenue rather than paying an outside vendor. The 2026 FIFA World Cup is a near-term catalyst: the 10-K shows $297 million in advance collections already received.

**The IMG agency** manages broadcast production and media rights distribution for third-party sports properties (Euroleague basketball, professional tennis, college sports). It is a services business with lower margins and no structural scarcity premium. Expert calls confirm the logic of keeping event management fees in-house, but the long-term fit of a diversified agency within an owned-IP company has been questioned.

**PBR** (Professional Bull Riders) is small and on-thesis — live, physical, year-round — but not needle-moving.

IMG grows at approximately 6% annually, which dilutes the consolidated growth rate from the 9-10% that UFC and WWE generate to roughly 8%. On Location adds real value. The agency business dilutes growth and margins.

## IV. The Macro Tailwind

Bureau of Economic Analysis data on live entertainment spending extends back to 1959. Four annual declines in 65 years: 1961 (minor recession), 2011 and 2012 (post-GFC hangover), and 2020 (COVID). The 2025 reading is $60.4 billion — an all-time high, 53% above the 2019 pre-COVID peak. The category has compounded at approximately 8.6% annually.

The post-COVID acceleration (15.2% CAGR from 2022-2025) reflects three converging forces. First, COVID demonstrated that digital content cannot substitute for being in the room — a preference shift, not a cyclical bounce. Second, the accelerating abundance of digital entertainment (streaming, social media, AI-generated content) makes the thing that is not on a screen more differentiated. Third, the industry discovered post-COVID that it had been systematically underpricing live events for decades. These forces are structural.

At $60 billion, live entertainment is just 0.27% of total personal consumption expenditures. Americans spend roughly $150 billion on home entertainment (streaming, cable, physical media) — a 2.5:1 ratio of screen-to-live spending. A shift in that ratio from 0.27% to 0.35% of PCE over a decade, on a growing base, implies $85-95 billion in annual live entertainment spending by 2035.

## VI. Corporate Structure

TKO is organized as an UP-C (Umbrella Partnership C-Corp). TKO Group Holdings, the listed entity, owns 40.1% of TKO OpCo. Endeavor/Silver Lake owns the remaining 59.9% through Class B partnership units. Silver Lake controls 61% of voting rights.

### The Tax Mechanics Are a Non-Issue

We traced the full distribution waterfall — through the 10-K cash flow statement and through sell-side models that independently calculate the Class A cash tracker — and confirmed zero variance between Class A free cash flow in the UP-C and what it would receive in a hypothetical normal C-corp.

The reported adjusted FCF of $1.2 billion imputes a 12-13% effective tax rate because OpCo is a partnership and partnership-level tax distributions show up in financing activities, not operations. This makes operating cash flow look $380M+ higher than a comparable C-corp. The fix is simple: apply a 25-29% normalized tax rate to pre-tax income and ignore the reported adjusted FCF metric. The per-share economics are identical either way.

### The Real Governance Concerns

**IMG dilution.** The February 2025 acquisition issued approximately 26.5 million Class B OpCo units to Endeavor, diluting Class A's economic interest from 49% to 40.1%. The 10-K states this caused "an approximately 6% reduction in the relative percentage interest" of other stockholders. This was a common control transaction — no fairness opinion, no shareholder vote. The timing (immediately before Silver Lake took Endeavor private in March 2025) raises questions about whose interests the transaction served.

**Silver Lake control.** Silver Lake appoints the CEO (Ari Emanuel, who runs both TKO and Endeavor simultaneously), controls the board, and can execute additional transactions without Class A approval. UP-C IPOs have historically underperformed comparable non-UP-C companies by 29% over three years (Billings et al., 2022, *Management Science*), driven primarily by governance and self-dealing dynamics rather than tax mechanics.

**Buyback accretion.** TKO returned $1.3 billion to shareholders in 2025 and announced another $1 billion program. Buybacks retire Class A shares while Class B stays fixed, which increases earnings per remaining Class A share. The per-share accretion works as intended. The percentage shift in economic interest (49% → 40.1%) is driven by the IMG unit issuance, not by buybacks.

The UP-C will likely simplify when Silver Lake seeks a full exit. Timeline is uncertain — could be 3 years, could be 7. Simplification would remove the governance discount.

## VII. Key Risks

**Fighter wage inflation.** UFC fighters earn approximately 20% of event revenue versus 45-55% in major US leagues and 70% in boxing. The Johnson v. Zuffa antitrust case targets 2010-2017 historical compensation and is expected to settle. The PFL competitive threat appears overstated — $100 million in Saudi funding is insufficient to systematically poach UFC's deep roster. The structural shift to watch: as UFC moves from PPV to guaranteed streaming revenue, fighters will negotiate for direct percentages of that revenue instead of PPV buy bonuses. A sustained move above 25% of event revenue compresses UFC margins 300-600 basis points.

**CBS viewership plateau.** If the simulcast flatlines at 2-3 million viewers, the 2033 renewal is more likely 1.2x than 1.5-2x. This reduces our 2036 media estimate by roughly $500 million and the base case enterprise value by $5-7 billion.

**Additional common control transactions.** Silver Lake has already executed one dilutive transaction. Further asset contributions or related-party dealings at non-market terms would confirm that Class A holders are a minority interest.

**Recession test.** Live entertainment spending grew through the 2008 recession but declined mildly in 2011-2012. We do not yet have a post-COVID recession data point to confirm whether the preference shift toward live experiences survives an income constraint.

**Premium pricing ceiling.** Aggressive ticket price increases risk alienating the casual fan base. The PPV-to-streaming transition mitigates this by expanding the top of the funnel, but the tension between maximizing per-event revenue and growing the audience is real and ongoing.

## Conclusion

TKO owns the two dominant year-round live sports franchises in an era where physical experiences are becoming structurally more scarce and more valuable. UFC and WWE compound at 9-10% annually on visible, largely contractual growth drivers. The macro tailwind — 65 years of near-uninterrupted compounding in live entertainment spending, still just 0.27% of total consumer spending — is durable. Our bottom-up event tracker gives us intra-quarter analytical visibility that consensus models do not replicate.

The corporate structure is complex and Silver Lake's control creates legitimate governance risk. The IMG acquisition diluted Class A without shareholder approval. These concerns are real. They are also known, and to a meaningful degree, priced — the stock trades at a discount to where a clean C-corp with these assets would trade.

At 19x EV/EBITDA, the valuation asks us to accept a fair multiple today and underwrite visible compounding from here. The 2033 Paramount+ renewal is the step-function catalyst. The CBS simulcast trajectory over the next three months will tell us whether that catalyst is 1.2x or 2x+. We have the tools to track it in real time.

Buy.
