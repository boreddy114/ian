## Summary

**Context.** Tyler is down ~36% over the past year and sits ~48% below its 2024 high, caught in the same software-complex de-rating that hit the group on AI-disruption fear. The fear has a specific edge here that it lacks for most names: expert commentary has fixated on the scenario of a small, AI-empowered team cheaply rebuilding government software and undercutting Tyler's municipal contracts. Meanwhile the business is accelerating — Q1'26 was a beat-and-raise, SaaS grew 23.5% (the 21st consecutive quarter above 20%), recurring revenue reached 87.8% of the total, and free cash flow more than doubled.

**Thesis.** Tyler is the scaled system-of-record for U.S. local and state government — courts, public safety, ERP, tax, and recording — serving a market of roughly 3,000 counties, 36,000 municipalities, and 12,600 school districts that it reaches through 45,000 installations. I judge the moat durable through AI because the barrier to displacing a Tyler core system was never the cost of writing the software. The binding constraints are the multi-year procurement cycle, the decades of accumulated records that must be converted and reconciled, and the political and operational risk of a failed migration of a system that runs payroll, dispatches police, or manages a court docket. Cheaper code does not touch any of those. The market is pricing Tyler as a legacy incumbent about to be disrupted; I see a compounding franchise running a cloud transition that deepens the lock-in while expanding margins.

**Price.** Tyler is out-of-favor but not cheap — ~25x forward non-GAAP earnings and ~19x EV/FCF after the drawdown, with richer GAAP optics (~45x trailing) carrying the NIC-amortization gap. The return is led by growth, not by a low multiple: durable double-digit recurring-revenue growth, margin expansion from the cloud transition, and buyback compound to low-to-mid-teens EPS growth, to which a partial re-rating adds as the AI-displacement fear is shown to be misplaced. I am underwriting the franchise and the growth algorithm, not a value multiple.

**Rating: Buy.** Proposed +25bps, in the core of the software re-entry. Conviction is a notch below VRSK — the moat question is genuinely more contested here, and the multiple offers less margin of safety — so the position is sized as a durable-growth holding rather than a deep-value one.

## Our Thesis

Tyler's government systems-of-record are protected by procurement, data-conversion, and mission-criticality barriers that AI does not erode, and the cloud transition deepens the lock-in while lifting margins. The franchise compounds recurring revenue at a double-digit rate and converts that to low-to-mid-teens EPS growth through margin expansion and buyback. At ~25x forward (≈19x EV/FCF) against a business growing recurring revenue ~10%+ with expanding margins, the market is pricing displacement risk I judge overstated for the mission-critical core. Falsifiable: if, in twelve months, the cloud flip has continued without elevated churn, cross-sell has advanced, and no AI-native competitor has taken a core system-of-record contract, the thesis holds.

## Thesis Tracker

1. **The cloud flip sustains, and clients re-contract rather than leave at the decision point.** 478 clients converted from on-prem to SaaS in the trailing year, and the flip is the moment a client could defect. The company stopped disclosing the quarterly flip count in Q1'26, so watch the dollar reads — ARR from new SaaS deals and conversions, total ARR, and SaaS bookings — together with net retention through the flip (management targets 80%+ on-cloud by 2030, with peak conversion 2027-2029). A flip that produces churn rather than upsell would break the core of the thesis.
2. **Cross-sell deepens from ~3 products toward 10-12.** The growth algorithm leans on selling more modules into an installed base that is migrating to cloud. Watch products-per-client and the state/federal cross-sell traction (the new dedicated state team). If attach stalls near current levels, the premium multiple loses its support.
3. **AI converts to billable revenue, and the vibe-coded threat stays theoretical.** Document Automation deals (Miami-Dade ~$800K, Harris County ~$1M) are early evidence AI is incremental revenue. Watch for AI deal momentum and, on the risk side, for any sign an AI-native competitor wins a core system-of-record contract — not a single department, but a courts, ERP, or public-safety core.

## Proof Points

*Trailing-180-day expert-call questions on Tyler cluster into a handful of concerns, with AI displacement and the durability of the switching-cost moat the most pressed. I worked each against the filings, the segment data, and the Q1'26 results.*

### Concern 1 — the switching-cost moat is procurement convenience, not real lock-in. My judgment: bifurcated, and durable where it matters.

This is the crux of the name, and the most credible bear case comes from a former Tyler product manager who argued clients buy multiple Tyler products because the procurement process makes it easier, not because the integration is superior. I take that seriously, because if the moat is mere convenience, an easier-to-deploy competitor erodes it. My judgment is that the bear is describing the contestable edge of the business and mistaking it for the core. The evidence is the cloud flip: 478 clients converted from on-prem licenses to SaaS over the trailing year, and the flip is precisely the decision point at which a client could defect — the contract is reopening, the migration effort is already on the table, and the budget is allocated. Clients are re-contracting on multi-year SaaS terms and getting upsold at that moment, not leaving. A moat that holds at its weakest point is a real moat. The segment data reinforces the bifurcation: the Enterprise Software core (courts, public safety, ERP, property) grew subscriptions 29% with SaaS up 26%, while the more contestable Platform Technologies layer was lumpy on a lost payment contract. The durable core is accelerating; the contestable edge is where the convenience critique actually bites, and it is the smaller part.

### Concern 2 — a vibe-coded AI competitor disintermediates the legacy incumbent. My judgment: cheaper code attacks a barrier that was never binding.

The most vivid concern in the expert calls is the scenario of twenty engineers generating a million lines of code and undercutting a Tyler contract in Des Moines. The premise is right that building government software is getting cheaper; the inference is wrong, because the cost of building was not the moat. Displacing a Tyler core system requires winning a multi-year procurement against an entrenched incumbent, converting decades of a jurisdiction's records into a new system and reconciling them to the satisfaction of auditors and regulators, and assuming the career and operational risk of a failed cutover on a system that cannot go down. None of those barriers gets cheaper because code generation got cheaper — they are procurement, data, and liability barriers, not engineering barriers. Tyler's own experience cuts the same way: it is using AI to compress its own data conversions from months to weeks, which speeds its migrations and, by extension, makes it a faster mover than a startup that still has to earn trust on a first reference deployment. The vibe-coded threat is real at the single-department, point-solution edge, where a nimble competitor can win a discrete workflow; it is weak against the integrated core.

### Concern 3 — the cloud transition and GovCloud will compress pricing power. My judgment: the flip is a pricing and margin tailwind, not a headwind.

The market worries that moving long-tenured clients to GovCloud resets pricing in the customer's favor. The data points the other way. Each flip converts a lower-value maintenance relationship into a higher-value SaaS subscription — maintenance revenue is declining as 478 clients moved off it, while SaaS grew 23.5%, and the net effect is higher revenue per client, not lower. Non-GAAP operating margin expanded to 27.2% even mid-transition, as cloud efficiency and version consolidation outpace the hosting cost of migration. The flip is also the natural upsell moment, which is why management frames it as the engine of the cross-sell motion. Pricing power in this market rests on the cost and risk of switching, which the flip does not reduce; it rests as well on the mission-criticality of the systems, which does not change in the cloud.

### Concern 4 — point-solution and data-first competitors (including Palantir) win at the edges. My judgment: real at the department level, not at the core.

This is where I concede the most ground, and it maps to the bifurcation. The expert calls cite best-of-breed startups winning single-department modernizations and name data-first players like Palantir and Snowflake appearing at the state level. I judge this a genuine but bounded threat: a city can modernize one department with a specialist without ripping out its Tyler ERP, and Tyler can lose a discrete workflow on usability. What is far harder is unseating the integrated back-office core, where the coordination across courts, finance, and public safety is the value. The competitive risk is to the growth rate at the margin, not to the installed base at the core — and Tyler's answer, deepening the integrated platform and adding AI to its own modules, is the right one if it executes.

### Concern 5 — AI is table stakes with no clear monetization. My judgment: early, but the first billable deals are real.

The skeptical version holds that government risk-aversion means AI features get demanded but not paid for. The early evidence contradicts it at the deal level: a Miami-Dade Document Automation deal closed at roughly $800K against a prior maintenance relationship near $250K, and a Harris County deal approached $1M, both sold on a labor-savings ROI rather than as free add-ons. Management is honest that the broader ramp will be slow — government moves slower than the private sector, and many AI features will be bundled into competitiveness rather than priced separately — so I treat AI as a measured contributor and an upside option, not a near-term growth driver. The Socrata acquisition (judicial intelligence, present in 45% of U.S. courtrooms) and the For The Record acquisition (legal-grade AI transcription) show the strategy is to buy and build AI on top of the proprietary government data and workflows Tyler already owns, which is the defensible place to put it.

## The Business

**How it makes money.** Tyler sells software and services to U.S. public-sector entities across two segments. Enterprise Software (~76% of revenue) is the mission-critical back-office core — courts and public safety, ERP/financials, property and recording, education. Platform Technologies (~24%) is the transaction layer — digital government services and payment processing, where revenue is partly volume-based and lumpier. Recurring revenue is 87.8% of the total and growing, split between SaaS subscriptions and a declining maintenance base that converts to SaaS as clients flip. ARR reached $2.15B, up ~10%. The model is shifting from perpetual-license-plus-maintenance to higher-value SaaS, which raises revenue per client and lifts margin as it scales.

**The earnings algorithm.** The Buy rests on converting double-digit recurring-revenue growth into low-to-mid-teens non-GAAP EPS growth, with the cloud transition doing double duty on both the revenue and margin lines.

| Driver | Contribution | Nature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Recurring revenue | ~+10% | Structural — SaaS-led, flip + cross-sell |
| Mix shift & margin | ~+2 to +3% | Structural — maintenance→SaaS, cloud efficiency |
| Buyback | ~+2 to +3% | Recurring — FCF-funded; buyback authorization ongoing |
| Tax / dilution | ~−1 to −2% | Cyclical — tax to ~23%+ on lower option benefit |
| Non-GAAP EPS | ~low-to-mid-teens | FY26E guide $12.50–12.75 (~10-12% on FTR-inclusive base) |

The structural distinction from a typical SaaS name is that Tyler's margin expansion is mechanical, not promised: each flip moves a client from maintenance to a richer SaaS contract while version consolidation and the shift off proprietary data centers cut delivery cost. Free cash flow margin is guided to 26-28% and more than doubled year-over-year in Q1. Buyback is funded entirely from free cash flow — the $600M convertible was repaid at maturity in Mar'26, after which Tyler opportunistically issued $1.44B of 0.50% notes due 2031 in May and used part of the proceeds to repurchase stock, so the balance sheet carries cheap, long-dated convertible debt broadly matched by cash.

**Competitive position and moat.** The 10-K describes the market as highly fragmented, and Tyler competing against consulting firms, smaller private vendors, and — tellingly — the internal IT departments of governments. That framing is the honest backdrop: Tyler is the scaled leader in a market with no single peer of comparable breadth, but it does not have a monopoly, and the fight is won account by account. The durable advantage is the combination of the broadest integrated product line in the sector, the installed base of 45,000 deployments that compounds cross-sell opportunity, and the switching costs detailed above. The reinvestment runway is the cross-sell from ~3 products toward 10-12 per client and the migration of the base to cloud, both of which extend the moat rather than merely defend it.

**Management and capital allocation.** Lynn Moore (CEO) and Brian Miller (CFO) have run a disciplined transition: repaying the $600M convertible in cash at maturity in March rather than diluting, then opportunistically issuing $1.44B of 0.50% notes due 2031 in May — capped calls lift the effective conversion price to ~$656 (a ~110% premium), and ~$321M of the proceeds repurchased ~1.0M shares at ~$312 concurrently — on top of the ~2.5% of shares already repurchased year-to-date near $315 under the $1B authorization, and acquiring adjacencies (For The Record, Socrata, several SaaS tuck-ins) that sit on top of the core government data and workflows. The buyback at these levels signals management views the drawdown as a value opportunity, consistent with their stated confidence in the 2030 plan and $1B+ free-cash-flow target. The capital-allocation watch item is acquisition discipline as the AI land-grab tempts richer multiples; the record so far is adjacent and reasonably priced.

**Valuation.** At ~$322 Tyler trades at ~25x FY26E and ~22x FY27E non-GAAP EPS, ~19x EV/FCF, and ~6x EV/sales; the GAAP multiples are richer (~39x forward, ~45x trailing) because amortization of the NIC-era intangibles depresses GAAP earnings. The balance sheet now carries the $1.44B 0.50% convert due 2031 issued in May, broadly offset by cash. This is out-of-favor quality, not value — the stock has de-rated ~36% and sits ~48% off its high, but the multiple still embeds a quality premium the franchise has historically earned. The return case does not require multiple expansion: low-to-mid-teens EPS growth sustained over three years compounds to a low-to-mid-teens annual return at a constant multiple. A partial re-rating, as the AI-displacement fear that compressed the stock proves overstated for the mission-critical core, is the upside. The risk the multiple carries is the mirror image — at ~25x forward there is less margin of safety if growth disappoints, which is why conviction sits a notch below the cheaper, slower VRSK.

## What Could Go Wrong

1. **The bear is right and the moat is convenience.** If the multi-product attach is procurement-driven inertia rather than genuine integration lock-in, an AI-native, easier-to-deploy competitor erodes the cross-sell engine that justifies the premium multiple. I judge the flip data argues against this, but I have management's framing and segment data, not former-customer accounts of attempting a full core switch. That evidence gap is the highest-value next step.
2. **A vibe-coded competitor takes a core contract.** The thesis holds that AI-native entrants win at the department edge, not the integrated core. A single credible loss of a courts, ERP, or public-safety system-of-record to an AI-native competitor would be a direct falsification and should change the rating, not just the estimate.
3. **The premium multiple offers little protection.** At ~25x forward, a growth disappointment — a stalled flip, a cross-sell plateau, or budget-driven sales-cycle elongation in a weaker funding environment — compresses the multiple from a high base. Unlike VRSK, there is no cheap valuation to cushion a stumble.
4. **Being wrong by omission.** As a Buy, the symmetric risk: if the cloud flip and AI monetization play out as management expects, a high-quality compounder bought at a 36% discount re-rates and a 25bps stake is too small. Confirmation on the flip and cross-sell would justify adding.

## Conclusion

Buy, +25bps, as a durable-growth holding in the software re-entry. Tyler is the scaled system-of-record for U.S. local government, protected by procurement, data, and mission-criticality barriers that AI does not erode, running a cloud transition that deepens the lock-in and expands margins. The market has discounted it ~36% on a displacement fear I judge overstated for the core. I would add on confirmation that the flip continues without churn and cross-sell advances; I would revisit the rating if an AI-native competitor takes a core system-of-record contract, or if the flip stalls. The June Investor Day and the next two prints — flip dollar metrics, cross-sell, and AI deal momentum — are the near-term decision points.
